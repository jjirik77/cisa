Section 03

Audit Planning & Charter Development

🗺️ Why Planning is the Most Critical Phase

Audit planning sets the entire engagement foundation. An auditor who fails to plan adequately risks incomplete coverage, missed risks, and findings that cannot withstand management scrutiny. The CISA exam expects you to think like a risk-based auditor — always asking: "What matters most, and where do I focus limited resources?"

Core Concept

Effective audit planning aligns audit objectives with organizational risk — not just with a pre-set checklist. The auditor must understand the business environment before determining scope.

Planning Steps

  • Understand the organization's IT environment and business context
  • Identify applicable laws, regulations, and standards
  • Review prior audit findings and management responses
  • Conduct preliminary risk assessment
  • Define audit universe and select audit subjects
  • Establish audit objectives, scope, and timeline
  • Assign team members and allocate resources
  • Prepare detailed audit work program

Key Planning Inputs

  • Organizational structure and IT governance policies
  • Risk registers and enterprise risk management (ERM) data
  • System inventories and data flow diagrams
  • Prior audit reports and corrective action plans
  • Regulatory requirements (SOX, HIPAA, GDPR, PCI-DSS)
  • Industry best practices (COBIT, ITIL, ISO 27001)
  • Internal audit charter and approved work plan
  • Budget constraints and staffing availability
💡

Exam Tip: When a question asks "what should the auditor do FIRST?" — the answer almost always relates to understanding the environment, reviewing the charter, or assessing risk. Never jump to testing before planning is complete.

📜 The Audit Charter Explained

The audit charter is the formal, board-approved document that gives the internal audit function its authority, independence, and mandate. Without a charter, an internal audit department lacks legitimacy.

Definition

An Audit Charter is a formal document that defines the purpose, authority, responsibility, and scope of the internal audit activity. It must be approved by the highest governance body (typically the audit committee of the board).

Charter Must Include

  • Purpose: Why the audit function exists
  • Authority: Right to access records, assets, personnel
  • Responsibility: Accountabilities and deliverables
  • Independence: Reporting line to audit committee
  • Objectivity: Freedom from management influence
  • Scope: Areas within audit's jurisdiction
  • Standards: Reference to ISACA IS Audit Standards

Independence Requirements

  • IS auditor should not audit systems they designed or operate
  • Organizational independence: report to audit committee, not IT mgmt
  • No financial or personal interest in audited systems
  • Rotate auditors on long-term engagements to preserve objectivity
  • Disclosure of any conflicts of interest before engagement begins
  • External auditors must be free from all client influence
⚡

Critical Point: Auditor independence is dual — organizational independence (where you report) AND individual objectivity (your own freedom from bias). Both must exist for valid audit results.

📐 IS Audit Standards & Frameworks

The IS auditor must work within established standards and leverage recognized frameworks for consistent, defensible audit work.

ISACA IS Audit Standards

  • 1001 — Audit Charter: Formal mandate required
  • 1002 — Organizational Independence
  • 1003 — Professional Independence
  • 1004 — Reasonable Expectation
  • 1005 — Due Professional Care
  • 1006 — Proficiency
  • 1007 — Assertions
  • 1008 — Criteria

Key Frameworks Used

  • COBIT 2019: Primary IT governance & management framework
  • ISO/IEC 27001: Information security management
  • ITIL v4: IT service management best practices
  • NIST CSF: Cybersecurity risk management
  • IIA Standards: Internal auditing professional standards
  • COSO: Internal control framework
COBIT for Auditors

COBIT's process maturity model allows auditors to rate IT processes on a 0–5 scale (Incomplete → Optimizing). An auditor uses this to set materiality thresholds and compare actual performance to target capability levels defined by management.

Section 04

Risk Assessment & Materiality

Risk-based auditing is the modern standard. Allocate effort where it matters most.

🎯 The Audit Risk Model

Audit risk = the risk of reaching an incorrect conclusion. Auditors must understand all three components:

Inherent Risk (IR)

The susceptibility of an area to material error without controls. Complex systems = higher IR. Cannot be reduced by the auditor.

Control Risk (CR)

Risk that existing internal controls fail to prevent or detect a material misstatement. Reduced by effective controls.

Detection Risk (DR)

Risk that auditor's own procedures fail to detect a material error. The only risk the auditor can directly control via testing rigor.

📏 Materiality in IS Auditing

Materiality is the threshold above which a finding is significant enough to influence the decisions of a reasonable user of the audit report. Auditors must set materiality thresholds before fieldwork begins.

  • Quantitative Materiality: Dollar value thresholds (e.g., 5% of revenue)
  • Qualitative Materiality: Nature of issue (e.g., regulatory violation regardless of dollar amount)
  • Performance Materiality: Lower threshold to catch accumulated smaller errors
  • Regulatory violations may be material even if financially small
  • Security breaches with reputational impact can be highly material
  • Materiality is re-evaluated throughout the engagement
  • Documented rationale must support materiality decisions
Section 05

Audit Evidence & Sampling

🔬 Qualities of Sufficient, Appropriate Evidence

Evidence is the backbone of every audit finding. Without reliable evidence, conclusions cannot be supported. ISACA requires evidence to meet the RACS criteria:

Relevant

Evidence must directly relate to the audit objective being tested.

Reliable

Comes from a credible, objective source. Independent external evidence is most reliable.

Complete

Evidence covers the entire audit period and population, not just favorable samples.

Sufficient

Enough evidence gathered to support the conclusion at an appropriate confidence level.

Evidence TypeDescriptionExampleReliability
ObservationAuditor directly witnesses a processWatching backup procedures performedHigh (direct)
InspectionExamination of records, documents, assetsReviewing firewall rule setsHigh
InquiryInterviews and questionnairesAsking IT staff about change proceduresLow-Medium
Re-performanceAuditor independently executes a controlRe-running a reconciliationVery High
ConfirmationThird-party verificationVendor confirming SLA complianceHigh (external)
AnalyticalComparison and trend analysisComparing access logs month over monthMedium
RecalculationVerifying mathematical accuracyRechecking automated report totalsHigh
🎯

Reliability Ranking: Re-performance > Inspection of external docs > Observation > Inspection of internal docs > Inquiry. On the exam, when asked for "most reliable" evidence, choose direct physical evidence or re-performance over verbal confirmation.

💻 Computer-Assisted Audit Techniques (CAATs)

CAATs allow auditors to test entire populations of data — eliminating sampling risk entirely. They are essential for modern IS audits and heavily tested on the CISA exam.

CAAT Categories

  • Audit Software (GAS): ACL/Galvanize, IDEA — query, analyze, and report on large datasets
  • Test Data: Auditor inputs known test transactions to verify system processing
  • Parallel Simulation: Reprocess client data in a controlled system and compare outputs
  • Embedded Audit Modules (EAM): Code built into production systems to capture data continuously
  • ITF (Integrated Test Facility): A dummy entity within the live system for continuous testing

When to Use CAATs

  • Large transaction volumes where manual review is impractical
  • Testing 100% of a population for specific criteria
  • Identifying duplicates, gaps, or outliers in data
  • Performing age analysis, stratification, or trend analysis
  • Confirming mathematical accuracy of calculations
  • Joining data across multiple systems to test completeness
⚡

Exam Tip: CAATs are preferred when data volumes are large, but require advance planning, client cooperation, and appropriate access controls during testing. The auditor must ensure CAATs don't contaminate production data.

CSA is a management-facilitated process where control owners and process owners collaboratively evaluate the effectiveness of controls in their area — guided (but not led) by the auditor.

Benefits: Raises control awareness, enables faster identification of control gaps, leverages management expertise, and creates ownership for remediation.

Working papers are property of the audit organization (not the client) and must be retained per the firm's records retention policy.

Section 07

Audit Reporting

📣 The Audit Report: Purpose & Structure

The audit report is the auditor's primary deliverable. It communicates findings, conclusions, and recommendations to management and the board. A well-written report drives change; a poorly written one is ignored.

Key Principle

Every finding must be supported by evidence documented in the working papers. The report should present findings in order of risk significance, with the most critical issues first. Avoid jargon; write for the intended audience.

Standard Report Components

  • Executive Summary: High-level conclusions for senior leadership
  • Scope & Objectives: What was audited and why
  • Methodology: How testing was conducted
  • Findings: Deficiencies identified with evidence
  • Risk Rating: Critical / High / Medium / Low for each finding
  • Recommendations: Specific, actionable remediation steps
  • Management Response: Auditee's agreed action plan
  • Follow-up Plan: Timeline and responsible parties

Findings Must Include (FARM)

  • F — Finding: What was observed (condition)
  • A — Attribute / Criteria: What should exist (standard)
  • R — Risk / Effect: Why it matters (impact)
  • M — Management Response: What will be done
💡

+ Cause: Some frameworks add the root cause — WHY the finding exists. Root cause analysis prevents recurrence vs. just patching symptoms.

🔄 Follow-Up & Monitoring

Reporting is not the end of the audit. The IS auditor has a professional responsibility to follow up on findings to ensure management remediated identified deficiencies.

  • Establish agreed target dates for each recommendation at time of report issuance
  • Conduct formal follow-up reviews at agreed intervals (typically 30/60/90 days)
  • Escalate unresolved critical findings to the audit committee
  • Document repeat findings — recurring issues signal systemic control failure
  • Consider continuous monitoring for high-risk areas
Section 08

Real-World Scenarios

Apply your knowledge to realistic IS audit situations. Reveal the auditor's recommended approach.

🏦 Scenario 1 — Banking IT Audit

The Independent Auditor Problem

During audit planning, you discover that the IS auditor assigned to review the core banking system was part of the team that implemented the system two years ago. The IS director argues that this makes the auditor uniquely qualified for the engagement and insists on keeping the assignment.

❓ What is the correct auditor response, and what standard applies?

The auditor must disclose the conflict of interest and be reassigned. Per ISACA Standard 1003 (Professional Independence), auditors cannot objectively review systems they helped design or implement — regardless of technical expertise. Independence in appearance is as important as independence in fact. The IS director's argument is not valid; specialized knowledge does not override independence requirements. The auditor should document the situation and escalate to the Chief Audit Executive (CAE) or audit committee if the IS director continues to insist.

🏥 Scenario 2 — Healthcare Compliance Audit

The CSA vs. Independent Audit Debate

A hospital's IT department proposes conducting a Control Self-Assessment (CSA) of their HIPAA security controls instead of an independent IS audit, arguing it will be faster, cheaper, and equally reliable since they know their systems best.

❓ How should the IS auditor evaluate this proposal?

CSA can complement an independent audit but cannot replace it for HIPAA compliance assurance. CSA is valuable for raising awareness and identifying self-identified gaps, but suffers from inherent bias — control owners evaluating their own controls. The IS auditor should: (1) Allow the CSA to proceed as a useful input, (2) Use CSA results to focus audit effort on areas management rated as low-risk (since these may have blind spots), (3) Conduct an independent review of the CSA process and outcomes. Regulators require independent third-party audit for formal HIPAA compliance attestation — CSA alone is insufficient.

🏢 Scenario 3 — SOX Integrated Audit

Evidence Sufficiency Challenge

During an SOX IT audit, you are testing the effectiveness of automated access controls over the financial reporting system. You select a sample of 25 user provisioning requests and find 2 exceptions — both involve terminated employees who retained access for 3 days after their termination date.

❓ How do you assess the significance of these findings and what should you do next?

An 8% exception rate (2/25) likely exceeds the tolerable error rate for a financially significant control (typically 0–5% for SOX). Steps: (1) Expand the sample — test additional items to determine whether the exception rate is isolated or systemic, (2) Assess impact — did the terminated employees access the system during the 3-day window? Review access logs, (3) Evaluate root cause — is this a process failure, system integration issue, or one-off error?, (4) Consider compensating controls — does a supervisor review access logs that would catch this?, (5) Conclude on control effectiveness — if the exception rate is confirmed as systemic, conclude the control is NOT effective and report accordingly to the audit committee. Never reduce sample size after finding exceptions.

Section 09

Key Terms Glossary

Click any term card to expand the full definition and exam context.

Audit Risk

The risk of reaching an incorrect audit conclusion

AR = Inherent Risk × Control Risk × Detection Risk. The auditor directly manages Detection Risk through the rigor of testing procedures. High inherent + high control risk demands low acceptable detection risk (more extensive testing).

🎯 Exam: "When IR and CR are both high, what should the auditor do?" → Increase substantive testing / lower detection risk.

Control Self-Assessment (CSA)

Management-facilitated internal control evaluation

A collaborative workshop process where management and process owners assess control design and effectiveness in their own area. The IS auditor facilitates but does not conduct the assessment. Results feed into, but do not replace, the independent audit.

🎯 Exam: CSA benefits include: management ownership, faster gap identification, and culture change — but it cannot substitute for independent auditing.

Integrated Auditing (IPA)

Combined financial and IT controls audit

An audit approach that simultaneously evaluates IT general controls (ITGC) and application controls alongside financial controls — recognizing that IT failures directly impact financial reporting reliability. Common in SOX audits.

🎯 Exam: IPA reduces duplication, improves efficiency, and gives holistic assurance that financial data integrity is maintained end-to-end.

Materiality

Threshold for what constitutes a significant finding

The magnitude of an omission or misstatement that makes it probable that a reasonable person would have made a different decision if informed of it. Can be quantitative (dollar threshold) or qualitative (regulatory impact, reputational risk).

🎯 Exam: A regulatory violation may be material even if financially immaterial — qualitative materiality applies.

Attribute Sampling

Statistical test for control compliance (yes/no)

Used in tests of controls to determine whether a specific attribute (e.g., "was the transaction properly authorized?") is present or absent. Results expressed as an estimated error rate with a confidence interval. Key inputs: tolerable error rate, expected error rate, confidence level.

🎯 Exam: Use attribute sampling for compliance testing (controls). Use variable sampling for substantive testing (values).

CAATs

Computer-Assisted Audit Techniques

Software-based tools that allow auditors to analyze entire data populations. Types: Generalized Audit Software (ACL, IDEA), Test Data, Parallel Simulation, Embedded Audit Modules (EAM), and Integrated Test Facility (ITF).

🎯 Exam: CAATs eliminate sampling risk by testing 100% of data — but require careful controls to avoid contaminating production systems.

Inherent Risk

Risk before any controls are applied

The susceptibility of an assertion, account, or process to a material misstatement, assuming no related controls exist. Driven by complexity, judgment, susceptibility to theft, and prior misstatements.

🎯 Exam: Auditors cannot reduce inherent risk — only controls and auditor testing procedures affect the overall audit risk equation.

Working Papers

Documented evidence supporting audit conclusions

The records maintained by the auditor to document procedures applied, evidence obtained, and conclusions reached. Must be sufficient to enable an experienced auditor not involved in the engagement to understand the work performed.

🎯 Exam: Working papers are the property of the auditing organization — not the client. Client access should be restricted and governed by policy.

COBIT 2019

IT governance and management framework

ISACA's primary framework for IT governance. Provides 40 governance and management objectives organized around 5 domains. Used by auditors to assess IT process maturity (Capability Levels 0–5) and identify gaps against target performance.

🎯 Exam: COBIT is the reference framework for IS audit standards. When a question asks which framework to use for assessing IT governance — COBIT is almost always the answer.

Detection Risk

Risk that audit tests miss a material error

The only component of audit risk that the auditor directly controls. Reduced by applying more rigorous testing procedures, larger sample sizes, or more effective audit techniques. Inversely related to combined inherent + control risk.

🎯 Exam: When asked how to reduce overall audit risk — the answer is to reduce detection risk through more extensive/rigorous testing.

Audit Universe

Complete inventory of all auditable entities

The complete list of all business processes, systems, departments, and activities that are within the scope of the internal audit function. The annual audit plan selects subjects from the audit universe based on risk assessment results.

🎯 Exam: The audit universe is maintained by the CAE, reviewed annually, and used to build the risk-based annual audit plan submitted to the audit committee.

Tolerable Error Rate (TER)

Maximum exception rate the auditor will accept

The highest rate of control deviations or dollar misstatements the auditor is willing to accept while still concluding the control is effective. Set during planning, before sampling. If actual exception rate exceeds TER, the control is not reliable.

🎯 Exam: TER is inversely related to sample size — lower TER → larger required sample. Financial reporting controls typically have TER of 0–3%.

🎓 What You've Mastered

Domain 1 represents the audit professional's complete toolkit — from the political authority of a charter to the granular mechanics of sampling and the strategic power of risk-based planning. Every other CISA domain ultimately asks: "How would the IS auditor evaluate this?" The answer always comes back to the principles you've studied here.

🧠 Core Themes to Remember

  • Auditor independence is non-negotiable — organizational AND individual
  • Risk-based approach: always ask "what matters most?"
  • Evidence hierarchy: re-performance > external > observation > inquiry
  • Detection risk is the only risk auditors can directly control
  • CSA supplements but never replaces independent auditing
  • COBIT is the primary IS governance reference framework
  • Working papers belong to the audit organization, not the client
  • Findings require: Condition, Criteria, Effect, Cause, Recommendation
