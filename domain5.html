About This Domain

What Auditors Must Know

Domain Scope

Domain 5 accounts for 25% of the CISA exam — roughly 38 questions. It is the most technically demanding domain and covers the full security control landscape.

  • Access Controls: IAM, PAM, authentication factors, authorisation models (RBAC, ABAC, MAC, DAC)
  • Cryptography: Symmetric vs. asymmetric, PKI, digital signatures, key management, hashing
  • Network Security: Firewalls, IDS/IPS, VPN, Zero Trust, network segmentation, DMZ
  • Physical Security: Data centre controls, environmental controls, visitor management
  • Incident Response: IR lifecycle, SIEM/SOAR, threat intelligence, deception technologies
  • Digital Forensics: Chain of custody, evidence preservation, forensic investigation process
  • Emerging Threats: Ransomware, supply chain attacks, social engineering, insider threats

The Auditor Mindset for Domain 5

Security auditing evaluates whether controls are adequate, operating effectively, and proportionate to risk. Key principles:

  • Controls are layered: No single control is sufficient. The auditor evaluates defence in depth — the combination of preventive, detective, and corrective controls.
  • Authentication ≠ Authorisation: Verifying who someone is (authentication) is separate from what they can do (authorisation). Both must be evaluated.
  • Encryption alone is not security: Encrypting data with weak key management, insecure algorithms, or poor certificate practices creates a false sense of security.
  • Forensic integrity is paramount: Evidence handling errors can make digital evidence inadmissible. The auditor evaluates chain of custody, not just findings.
  • Zero Trust means verify always: "Never trust, always verify" — location inside the network does not grant inherent trust.
  • SIEM without response is theatre: Detection without documented response procedures and trained personnel is an incomplete control.
💡

Exam tip: Domain 5 questions often test the auditor's validation role — not whether the organisation has security tools, but whether those tools are correctly configured, monitored, and integrated into a coherent security programme.

Section 3

Access Control

Identity & Access Management (IAM)

IAM governs who has access to what, when, and under what conditions. The auditor evaluates the full identity lifecycle.

  • Joiner: Provisioning — access granted based on role, with manager approval; principle of least privilege applied from day one
  • Mover: Re-provisioning — when role changes, old access revoked before new access granted; cumulative access (privilege creep) is a finding
  • Leaver: De-provisioning — access revoked on termination date, not weeks later; emergency revocation process for involuntary terminations
  • Orphan accounts: Active accounts with no associated active employee — critical finding; common after M&A, restructuring
  • Access recertification: Periodic review (quarterly for privileged, annual for standard) where managers certify each employee's access is still appropriate
  • SoD enforcement: IAM system must enforce Segregation of Duties — user cannot be assigned conflicting roles (e.g., payment initiation + payment approval)

Privileged Access Management (PAM)

PAM controls the highest-risk accounts in the environment — administrators, DBAs, system accounts, and service accounts with elevated permissions.

  • Just-in-time (JIT) access: Privileged access granted for a specific task window, then automatically revoked — no standing admin accounts
  • Privileged session recording: All privileged sessions recorded and stored securely — DBA and admin activity fully auditable
  • Password vaulting: Privileged credentials stored in a PAM vault, rotated automatically after each use — no shared admin passwords
  • Break-glass accounts: Emergency admin accounts with dual-key activation (two people required), immediate alert on use, mandatory PIR
  • Service account governance: Non-human accounts must have an owner, documented purpose, and periodic review
  • Auditor check: Can admins access the PAM vault logs? If yes — critical finding (self-auditing)

MFA Requirements

  • Two different factor types: Two passwords ≠ MFA. Must combine factors from different categories (e.g., password + authenticator app)
  • SMS OTP risk: SMS is the weakest MFA method — vulnerable to SIM-swapping. TOTP authenticator apps or hardware tokens preferred
  • Phishing-resistant MFA: FIDO2/WebAuthn hardware keys are immune to credential phishing — recommended for privileged accounts
  • Adaptive MFA: Risk-based authentication that triggers additional factors based on anomalous signals (new device, unusual location, off-hours)

Single Sign-On (SSO) & Federation

  • SSO: One authentication grants access to multiple applications — reduces password fatigue and enables central de-provisioning
  • Federation: SAML, OAuth 2.0, and OpenID Connect enable identity to be asserted across organisational boundaries
  • SSO risk: Single point of failure — compromise of IdP compromises all connected applications; MFA on the IdP is essential
  • Auditor check: Are all applications connected to SSO, or do some maintain separate credentials (shadow accounts)?

The auditor evaluates whether roles are properly defined, whether SoD is enforced across roles, and whether access recertification is performed. Privilege creep (accumulating access from multiple roles over time without removing old access) is the most common RBAC finding.

Cryptography

Toggle between encryption paradigms and explore PKI controls.

Symmetric Encryption

Same key used to encrypt and decrypt. Fast and efficient for bulk data encryption.

  • AES-256: Current standard — approved by NIST, used by US government for classified data. Block cipher, 256-bit key.
  • AES-128: Also secure for most purposes — faster than AES-256 with minimal practical security difference
  • 3DES (Triple DES): Legacy — applying DES three times. Deprecated by NIST in 2023; should not be used in new systems
  • DES: Broken — 56-bit key, cracked in under 24 hours with modern hardware. Never use.
  • Key distribution problem: How do you securely share the secret key? Solved by using asymmetric encryption to exchange the symmetric key (hybrid encryption)

Symmetric — Audit Checklist

  • Is AES-256 (or AES-128 minimum) used for data at rest?
  • Are deprecated algorithms (3DES, DES, RC4) still in use? — finding if yes
  • Are encryption keys stored separately from the data they protect?
  • Is there a documented key rotation schedule?
  • Are keys protected in a Hardware Security Module (HSM) or key management system?
  • Is data encrypted in transit AND at rest — or only one?
  • Are encryption standards formally documented in a cryptography policy?

Asymmetric Encryption

Key pair: public key (share freely) + private key (keep secret). Solves the key distribution problem.

  • RSA: Most widely used asymmetric algorithm. Minimum 2048-bit key; 4096-bit recommended for long-term security. Based on difficulty of factoring large primes.
  • ECC (Elliptic Curve): Provides equivalent security to RSA with much shorter keys (256-bit ECC ≈ 3072-bit RSA). Used in TLS 1.3, mobile devices.
  • Encrypt with public key → decrypt with private key: Only the key holder can decrypt. Used for secure key exchange and TLS.
  • Sign with private key → verify with public key: Proves the message came from the key holder. Used for digital signatures.
  • Hybrid encryption: Asymmetric encrypts the symmetric session key; symmetric encrypts the bulk data — combines both paradigm strengths.

Digital Signatures

A digital signature provides authentication, integrity, and non-repudiation.

  • Process: Sender hashes the message → encrypts hash with private key → attaches as signature
  • Verification: Receiver decrypts signature with sender's public key → independently hashes message → compares hashes
  • Authentication: Only the private key holder could have signed — proves identity
  • Integrity: Any modification to the message changes the hash — signature verification fails
  • Non-repudiation: Signer cannot deny having signed — private key ownership is provable
  • Audit finding: Signing with MD5 or SHA-1 is a finding — both are cryptographically broken; SHA-256 minimum required

Cryptographic Hashing

One-way function producing a fixed-length digest. Used for integrity verification, password storage, and digital signatures.

  • SHA-256: Current standard — 256-bit output, collision-resistant, approved for all security applications
  • SHA-3: Alternative to SHA-2 family — different design basis, equally secure, used where SHA-2 diversity is desired
  • SHA-1: Broken — practical collision attacks demonstrated. Finding if still in use for any security purpose
  • MD5: Broken — widely exploited. Finding in any security context including password storage, certificate signing, file integrity
  • bcrypt / Argon2: Password hashing — deliberately slow to resist brute-force, includes salt to prevent rainbow tables. SHA-256 is NOT suitable for password storage.
  • HMAC: Hash-based Message Authentication Code — combines hashing with a secret key to provide both integrity and authentication

Hashing — Audit Checklist

  • Are passwords stored as salted hashes using bcrypt, Argon2, or PBKDF2? (Plaintext or unsalted = critical finding)
  • Is SHA-1 or MD5 used anywhere for security purposes? — finding
  • Are file integrity checks using SHA-256 or better?
  • Is HMAC used for API message authentication — not just plain hashing?
  • Are TLS certificates signed with SHA-256 or better?
  • Is there a cryptography inventory identifying all algorithms in use across systems?
Critical Distinction

Hashing is NOT encryption. A hash cannot be reversed. Use hashing for integrity and password storage. Use encryption when data must be decrypted later.

Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)

PKI is the framework of policies, procedures, and technology that manages digital certificates and public keys.

  • CA (Certificate Authority): Issues and signs digital certificates — vouches for the binding of a public key to an identity
  • Root CA: The trust anchor. Typically kept offline (air-gapped) and used only to sign Intermediate CAs
  • Intermediate CA: Online CA that issues end-entity certificates — compromise is severe but contained; Root CA revokes and reissues
  • Certificate: Binds a public key to an identity (X.509 format). Contains: subject, public key, validity period, issuing CA, serial number, signature
  • CRL (Certificate Revocation List): Published list of revoked certificates — must be checked before trusting a certificate
  • OCSP (Online Certificate Status Protocol): Real-time certificate status check — faster than CRL, preferred for high-assurance environments
  • Certificate Transparency (CT): Public log of all issued certificates — enables detection of misissued or fraudulent certificates

PKI — Audit Checklist

  • Is the Root CA kept offline and physically secured?
  • Are certificate validity periods appropriate? (TLS max 398 days per CA/Browser Forum)
  • Is there a certificate inventory — does anyone know what certificates exist and when they expire?
  • Is there an automated certificate renewal process? (Manual renewal = expiry risk)
  • Are expired or self-signed certificates in use in production? — finding
  • Is CRL or OCSP validation enabled on all systems that trust external certificates?
  • Are private keys stored in HSMs — not in software keystore files?
  • Is there a defined process for certificate revocation and re-issuance when a key is compromised?
Section 5

Network Security

Network Segmentation & DMZ

Network segmentation isolates systems to contain the blast radius of a breach. The DMZ (Demilitarised Zone) is a perimeter network for publicly accessible services.

  • DMZ: Sits between the external internet and the internal network — hosts web servers, email gateways, DNS. Compromise of a DMZ system does not automatically grant internal network access.
  • Micro-segmentation: Granular segmentation within internal networks — east-west traffic between servers is controlled, not just north-south traffic at the perimeter
  • VLAN: Virtual LAN — logical network segmentation at Layer 2. Limits broadcast domains and contains lateral movement
  • Network zones: Internet → DMZ → Internal → Restricted (e.g., PCI zone). Each boundary controlled by a firewall or ACL
  • Flat network finding: All systems on the same subnet with no segmentation — a breach of any system enables lateral movement to all systems. Critical finding.

Network Security — Audit Questions

  • Is there a current, accurate network diagram showing all zones and traffic flows?
  • Are firewall rules reviewed and recertified at least annually?
  • Are there any "any-to-any" or "any port" firewall rules? — finding
  • Is the internal network flat, or is there segmentation between business units and sensitive systems?
  • Are production, development, and test environments on separate network segments?
  • Are there controls preventing sensitive data from leaving the network (DLP)?
  • Is network traffic encrypted on internal segments carrying sensitive data?

Firewall Types

  • Packet filtering: Inspects IP headers only — source/destination IP and port. Fast but cannot inspect payload or track connection state.
  • Stateful inspection: Tracks connection state — knows whether a packet is part of an established session. Most common perimeter firewall type.
  • Application-layer (WAF): Inspects HTTP/HTTPS payload — detects SQL injection, XSS, OWASP Top 10 attacks at Layer 7. Required for web applications.
  • NGFW (Next-Generation): Combines stateful + application awareness + IPS + SSL inspection + threat intelligence feeds.
  • Implicit deny: All traffic blocked unless explicitly permitted — the correct default stance. "Permit all, deny specific" is a finding.

IDS vs. IPS

  • IDS (Intrusion Detection System): Detects and alerts — passive monitoring. Does not block traffic. Out-of-band deployment.
  • IPS (Intrusion Prevention System): Detects and blocks — active inline. Can drop malicious traffic in real time.
  • Signature-based: Matches traffic against known attack patterns. Fast, low false positive, cannot detect zero-day attacks.
  • Anomaly-based: Detects deviations from baseline behaviour. Can detect novel attacks but higher false positive rate.
  • Placement: IDS/IPS placed at network boundaries AND between internal segments for east-west visibility.
  • Auditor check: Are IPS signatures updated? When were they last updated? Outdated signatures = effectively no detection.

Zero Trust Architecture

Zero Trust replaces the traditional perimeter model with continuous verification. The principle: "Never trust, always verify." Being inside the network does not grant implicit trust.

Verify Explicitly
Always Authenticate & Authorise

Every access request is authenticated and authorised regardless of network location. No implicit trust from being on-site or VPN-connected.

Least Privilege
Minimum Access Required

Access granted to the minimum resources needed for the specific task. JIT access for elevated permissions. Time-limited access grants.

Assume Breach
Design for Compromise

Assume attackers are already inside. Micro-segmentation limits blast radius. End-to-end encryption even on internal networks. Continuous monitoring.

Device Trust
Verify Device Health

Device compliance checked before granting access — patch level, AV status, certificate. Unmanaged or non-compliant devices quarantined.

Micro-segmentation
Control East-West Traffic

Workloads isolated — even within the internal network, lateral movement requires explicit authorisation. Database cannot be reached from a compromised web server.

Continuous Monitoring
Log & Inspect Everything

All traffic inspected and logged. Anomalies detected in real time. Session behaviour analysed continuously — not just at authentication time.

💡

Exam tip: Zero Trust is a design philosophy, not a product. An organisation can purchase a "Zero Trust" product and still have a poor Zero Trust posture if implementation is incomplete. The auditor evaluates the actual controls — micro-segmentation, MFA, device health verification, continuous monitoring — not just the vendor's marketing claims.

VPN Controls

  • IPsec VPN: Network-layer encryption — encrypts all traffic between sites or between device and gateway. Used for site-to-site connectivity.
  • TLS/SSL VPN: Application-layer — accessed via browser or thin client. More flexible, easier to manage for remote users.
  • Split tunnelling: Only corporate traffic goes through VPN; internet traffic goes direct. Risk: device may be exposed to threats on local network while connected to corporate resources.
  • Full tunnel: All traffic routed through VPN — more secure but higher bandwidth demand on corporate infrastructure.
  • MFA on VPN: Mandatory. Credential-only VPN authentication is insufficient — remote access is a primary target for credential-based attacks.
  • VPN access reviews: Who has VPN access? Is it scoped to necessary resources? Are inactive VPN accounts disabled?

Remote Access Audit Controls

  • Device posture assessment: Only managed, compliant devices granted VPN/remote access — not personal devices without controls
  • Session timeout: Idle remote sessions automatically terminated — prevents unattended sessions from being hijacked
  • Privileged remote access: Admin access over remote sessions must use PAM-gated connections — no direct RDP/SSH with shared credentials
  • Third-party remote access: Vendor remote connections must be time-limited, monitored, and scoped to minimum required systems
  • Audit logging: All remote sessions logged — who, from where, when, what systems accessed

Physical Security

Data Centre Physical Controls

Physical security is the foundation of all other security controls — a physically accessible server cannot be protected by logical controls alone.

  • Perimeter security: Fencing, security guards, CCTV covering all entry/exit points and aisles. CCTV footage retained minimum 90 days.
  • Mantrap / airlock: Double-door entry system — first door must close before second opens. Prevents tailgating. Used at data centre entrances.
  • Multi-factor physical access: Badge + PIN, or badge + biometric — prevents badge cloning from granting full access
  • Visitor management: All visitors logged, escorted at all times, never left alone in server areas. No exceptions for "trusted" vendors.
  • Rack-level access: Individual server racks locked — access logged. Even data centre staff should not have access to racks outside their responsibility.
  • Two-person integrity: Sensitive operations (e.g., hardware removal, tape handling) require two authorised people — prevents insider theft
  • Clean desk / clear screen: Sensitive documents not left unattended; workstations lock after inactivity

Environmental & Media Controls

  • Temperature/humidity: Servers operate in narrow temperature/humidity range — monitoring with automated alerts, redundant cooling (N+1 minimum)
  • UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply): Battery backup for clean power during outages — buys time for generators to start
  • Generator: Extended power backup — tested under load quarterly. Fuel supply and delivery contract audited.
  • Fire suppression: Clean agent systems (FM-200, Novec) safe for electronics — not water. CO₂ rooms require evacuation warning before discharge.
  • Flood/water detection: Sensors under raised floors and around cooling units — alert before water reaches equipment
  • Media sanitisation: Data bearing media destroyed or degaussed before disposal. Certificate of destruction obtained from vendors. NIST SP 800-88 guidance.
  • Tape/backup media: Encrypted before leaving the building. Chain of custody maintained for offsite storage and retrieval.

SIEM — Security Information & Event Management

SIEM aggregates and correlates security events from across the environment to detect threats that no single source would reveal.

  • Log aggregation: Centralises logs from firewalls, servers, endpoints, identity systems, applications, cloud platforms
  • Correlation rules: Detects multi-step attacks — e.g., failed login from IP X, then successful login from IP X, then large file transfer
  • Alert management: High-fidelity alerts require tuning — too many false positives leads to alert fatigue and missed real incidents
  • Retention: Minimum 12 months online, 7 years archive for regulated industries
  • Tamper protection: SIEM logs must be write-protected — systems cannot delete or modify their own log entries in the SIEM
  • Auditor question: Are SIEM alerts reviewed within SLA? By whom? What is the escalation path for unreviewed alerts?

SOAR — Security Orchestration, Automation & Response

SOAR automates repetitive incident response tasks, enabling the security team to focus on complex investigations.

  • Playbooks: Automated, predefined response workflows triggered by SIEM alerts — e.g., phishing email detected → block sender, quarantine email, notify user, pull email headers, check URL in threat intel
  • Enrichment: Automatically enriches alerts with context — IP reputation, user risk score, asset criticality, threat intelligence
  • Orchestration: Coordinates actions across tools — firewall block, endpoint isolation, ticketing system creation, management notification — in seconds
  • Metrics: SOAR enables measurement of MTTR (Mean Time to Respond) and MTTC (Mean Time to Contain)
  • Auditor check: Are SOAR playbooks reviewed and tested? Can they be triggered incorrectly — causing false positive containment of legitimate traffic?

Threat Intelligence

Threat intelligence enables organisations to anticipate, detect, and respond to threats relevant to their environment and industry.

  • Strategic intelligence: High-level — threat actor motivations, geopolitical context, industry-level trends. Used by CISO and board for risk decisions.
  • Tactical intelligence: TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, Procedures) used by threat actors. MITRE ATT&CK framework maps known adversary behaviours.
  • Operational intelligence: Active campaigns and specific attack details. Shared in ISACs (Information Sharing and Analysis Centers) for sector-specific threats.
  • Technical intelligence: IOCs (Indicators of Compromise) — specific IPs, domains, file hashes, signatures. Directly actionable in SIEM and EDR.
  • Deception technologies: Honeypots (fake systems), honeytokens (fake credentials), canary files — attract attackers and generate high-confidence alerts when accessed
  • Honeypot audit value: A honeypot access event = near-certain malicious activity — high-fidelity detection with almost no false positives

MITRE ATT&CK Framework

The MITRE ATT&CK framework catalogues adversary tactics and techniques observed in real-world attacks. Auditors use it to assess detection coverage.

  • Tactics: The "why" — adversary goals (Initial Access, Execution, Persistence, Privilege Escalation, Defense Evasion, Credential Access, Discovery, Lateral Movement, Exfiltration, Impact)
  • Techniques: The "how" — specific methods used to achieve each tactic (e.g., T1566 Phishing for Initial Access)
  • Audit use: Map existing detection rules against ATT&CK — identify which techniques have no detection coverage ("detection gaps")
  • Red team alignment: Penetration testers use ATT&CK to simulate realistic adversary behaviour — results identify actual gaps vs. theoretical coverage

Digital Forensics

Forensic Investigation Principles

Digital forensics produces evidence that may be used in legal proceedings or disciplinary actions. Procedure errors can render evidence inadmissible.

  • Locard's Exchange Principle: Every contact leaves a trace — even accessing a system to investigate changes it. Forensic tools minimise write operations.
  • Order of volatility: Collect most volatile evidence first — registers/cache → RAM → running processes → network connections → disk → backups. RAM is lost when system powers off.
  • Write blockers: Hardware devices that prevent any write operations to the evidence drive during imaging — preserve original state
  • Forensic image: Bit-for-bit copy of entire storage media — including deleted files, slack space, unallocated clusters. NOT just a file copy.
  • Hash verification: SHA-256 hash of original and image must match — proves copy is exact and unaltered
  • Working copy: All analysis conducted on a copy — original evidence never touched during analysis

Chain of Custody

Chain of custody documents the complete chronological record of who had access to evidence and what was done with it. Breaks in chain of custody can invalidate evidence.

  • Evidence documentation: Each piece of evidence labelled with: case number, date/time collected, collector identity, description, hash values
  • Tamper-evident sealing: Evidence bags or containers sealed with tamper-evident tape — any breach is visible
  • Transfer log: Every transfer of custody documented — signature from both parties, date, time, reason
  • Secure storage: Evidence stored in locked, access-controlled location — access log maintained
  • Admissibility risk: Unaccounted gaps in chain of custody allow defence to argue evidence was tampered with — potential dismissal of case
  • Auditor note: Even internal investigations should follow chain of custody — disciplinary cases may later become criminal proceedings
Forensics Investigation Process

1. Identification — Identify potential evidence sources (devices, cloud, logs)  →  2. Preservation — Secure and freeze evidence, prevent modification  →  3. Collection — Acquire forensic image with hash verification  →  4. Examination — Apply forensic tools to extract relevant data  →  5. Analysis — Interpret findings in context of the investigation  →  6. Reporting — Document findings in a clear, defensible report suitable for legal or disciplinary proceedings

Real-World Scenarios

Choose the correct auditor response, then read the expert explanation.

Scenario 1 — Access Controls

The Finance Director's Privileges

During an access control audit, you discover the Finance Director has accumulated system permissions across six financial applications over eight years of role changes — including permissions from her previous roles as an Accounts Payable Clerk and Payroll Analyst. She now has the ability to: create suppliers, process payments, approve payments, and view payroll data — all in the same systems.

What is the auditor's PRIMARY finding and recommended action?

This is a critical finding combining two issues: (1) Privilege creep — accumulated permissions from past roles that should have been revoked during each role transition. This is the most common access control finding in enterprise environments. (2) SoD violation — the ability to create suppliers AND initiate AND approve payments in the same system is the classic fraud-enabling permission combination. This combination enables a single person to create a fictitious vendor, submit fraudulent invoices, and approve their own payments with no requirement for any other person's involvement. The auditor should recommend: immediate revocation of permissions from prior roles, formal access recertification process, SoD matrix defining conflicting permissions, and quarterly access reviews with manager certification. MFA does not compensate for SoD violations — it authenticates who the user is, but does not prevent abuse of legitimately authenticated sessions.

Scenario 2 — Cryptography

The Certificate Situation

Your organisation's IT team uses self-signed certificates on all internal web applications. They argue this is acceptable because "these are internal systems, not internet-facing, so certificate authority trust doesn't matter." Additionally, the customer-facing web application uses a certificate with SHA-1 signing and a validity period of 3 years. The DBA confirms database connections use an old SSL 3.0 configuration that "has always worked."

How many distinct findings does this situation contain?

There are four distinct findings: (1) Self-signed certificates on internal systems — self-signed certificates cannot be validated against a trusted CA. Users and systems must bypass certificate warnings, training users to ignore security alerts. Internal PKI (an internal CA) should be used instead. (2) SHA-1 certificate signing — SHA-1 is cryptographically broken; practical collision attacks have been demonstrated. All certificates must use SHA-256 minimum. (3) 3-year certificate validity — the CA/Browser Forum maximum for public TLS certificates is 398 days (~13 months). A 3-year certificate likely predates the current standard and may not support modern certificate features. (4) SSL 3.0 on database connections — SSL 3.0 was deprecated in 2015 (RFC 7568) due to the POODLE vulnerability. TLS 1.2 minimum, TLS 1.3 preferred. "It has always worked" is not a security justification — it means the vulnerability has been present for years.

Scenario 3 — Incident Response

The Breach Notification Dilemma

A penetration tester discovers a database containing 50,000 EU customer records is exposed on a public-facing server with no authentication — it has been publicly accessible for an unknown period. The IT manager says: "We don't know if anyone actually accessed the data, so there's no confirmed breach, and we don't need to notify anyone until we have evidence of actual access." It is now Day 2 since discovery. The organisation is subject to GDPR.

What should the auditor advise?

Under GDPR Article 33, the supervisory authority must be notified within 72 hours of the organisation becoming aware of a personal data breach — this is already Day 2 of that 72-hour window. Key points: (1) Availability breach = personal data breach. GDPR defines a personal data breach as any breach of security leading to accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorised disclosure of, or access to personal data. An unauthenticated, publicly accessible database is an unauthorised disclosure risk — the breach is the lack of access control, not necessarily proven exfiltration. (2) Unknown access period increases severity. The unknown exposure window means the organisation cannot rule out access — this typically triggers notification obligations. (3) The auditor should escalate immediately to DPO and legal counsel — notification must be submitted within the remaining window, and data subject notification may also be required (GDPR Article 34) if the breach is likely to result in high risk to individuals. Waiting for complete forensic certainty before notifying has resulted in significant GDPR fines.

IAM / PAM

Identity & Access Management / Privileged Access Management — governing who has access and protecting the highest-risk accounts.

IAM manages the full identity lifecycle (joiner/mover/leaver), access provisioning, recertification, and SoD enforcement. PAM specifically controls privileged accounts (admins, DBAs) through password vaulting, just-in-time access, session recording, and break-glass procedures. Both are evaluated by auditors for every significant IT audit.

🎯 Exam: Privilege creep (accumulated excess access) and orphan accounts (active accounts with no owner) are the most common IAM findings. PAM key finding: admins who can access their own session recordings or PAM logs.
✓ Known

Zero Trust

"Never trust, always verify" — security model eliminating implicit trust based on network location.

Zero Trust requires continuous verification of identity, device health, and request context for every access attempt — regardless of whether the request comes from inside or outside the network. Core pillars: verify explicitly, use least privilege, assume breach. Implemented through micro-segmentation, MFA everywhere, continuous monitoring, and device posture assessment.

🎯 Exam: Zero Trust is a philosophy and architecture, not a product. The auditor evaluates actual controls (MFA, segmentation, monitoring) not vendor claims. Being on the corporate network does not grant trust in a Zero Trust model.
✓ Known

Symmetric vs. Asymmetric Encryption

Symmetric: same key for encrypt/decrypt. Asymmetric: key pair (public/private) for different operations.

Symmetric (AES): fast, efficient for bulk data, key distribution is the challenge. Asymmetric (RSA, ECC): slower, solves key distribution, enables digital signatures. Hybrid: asymmetric exchanges the symmetric session key; symmetric encrypts the data — combines strengths of both. AES-256 and RSA-2048 (minimum) are current standards. DES, 3DES, RC4 are deprecated findings.

🎯 Exam: AES-256 for data at rest and in transit. RSA-2048 minimum for asymmetric. DES/3DES = finding. SHA-256 minimum for hashing. MD5/SHA-1 = finding in any security context.
✓ Known

PKI / CRL / OCSP

Public Key Infrastructure — framework for managing digital certificates, certificate revocation, and trust.

PKI: CA hierarchy (Root CA offline → Intermediate CA → End-entity certificates). CRL: published list of revoked certificates — must be checked before trusting. OCSP: real-time certificate status check — preferred over CRL. Certificate Transparency: public log enabling detection of fraudulent certificates. Audit focus: Root CA offline, certificate inventory, automated renewal, no self-signed in production, private keys in HSM.

🎯 Exam: Self-signed certificates = finding. Expired certificates = finding. SHA-1 signed certificates = finding. Root CA online and connected = critical finding. CRL/OCSP not validated = finding.
✓ Known

MFA

Multi-Factor Authentication — combining two or more different factor types to verify identity.

Factors: something you know (password), something you have (authenticator app, hardware key), something you are (biometric). Two different types required — two passwords is NOT MFA. SMS OTP is weakest (SIM-swap vulnerable). FIDO2/WebAuthn hardware keys are phishing-resistant and strongest. Adaptive MFA triggers additional factors based on risk signals. MFA is mandatory for privileged accounts, VPN, and any internet-facing access.

🎯 Exam: Password + SMS = MFA (though weak). Password + second password = NOT MFA. The auditor checks MFA covers privileged accounts, remote access, and admin consoles — not just general user logins.
✓ Known

RBAC / DAC / MAC / ABAC

Four access control models governing how authorisation decisions are made.

DAC: owner-controlled — flexible but inconsistent. MAC: system-enforced labels — rigid, government/military. RBAC: roles define permissions, users assigned to roles — most common enterprise model, privilege creep risk. ABAC: attribute-based policy — most flexible and fine-grained, complex to audit. The auditor evaluates whether the chosen model is consistently applied, whether SoD is enforced, and whether access recertification is performed.

🎯 Exam: RBAC is the most tested model. Privilege creep = most common finding. SoD enforcement within RBAC is critical for financial systems. MAC is used for classified government information — rigid, not user-configurable.
✓ Known

Chain of Custody

Documented chronological record of who possessed evidence, when, and what was done with it.

Chain of custody ensures digital evidence is legally admissible. Every transfer of evidence requires signed documentation from both parties. Evidence must be stored in tamper-evident, access-controlled conditions. Hash verification (SHA-256) confirms the forensic image matches the original. Any gap in chain of custody — missing signatures, undocumented transfers, unsecured storage — allows defence to challenge evidence integrity and seek dismissal.

🎯 Exam: Internal investigations should still follow chain of custody — disciplinary matters can become criminal proceedings. The auditor does not touch original evidence — all analysis on forensic copy. Order of volatility: collect RAM before disk before logs.
✓ Known

Honeypot / Honeytoken

Deception technologies that attract attackers and generate high-confidence alerts on access.

Honeypot: fake system (server, workstation) with no legitimate use — any connection is suspicious. Honeytoken: fake credential (username/password, API key, file) — any use triggers an alert. Canary file: a file that alerts when accessed. These are high-fidelity detection tools — legitimate users never interact with them, so any access is near-certain attacker activity. Auditors evaluate whether these are deployed and whether alerts are actively monitored.

🎯 Exam: Honeypots detect, they do not prevent. They are a detective control. Access to a honeypot = near-certain indicator of compromise with minimal false positives — highest confidence detection in most security programmes.
✓ Known

SIEM / SOAR

SIEM: centralised security log correlation and alerting. SOAR: automated incident response orchestration.

SIEM aggregates logs from across the environment, correlates events to detect threats, and generates alerts. SOAR automates response through playbooks — enriches alerts, orchestrates tool actions, measures MTTR. Key audit questions: Are SIEM alerts monitored 24/7? Are response SLAs defined and measured? Are SOAR playbooks tested? Can systems delete their own log entries in the SIEM? (If yes — critical finding.) A SIEM with no alert review process is not a security control.

🎯 Exam: SIEM = detection. SOAR = automated response. Both require operational effectiveness assessment — the tool existing is not sufficient; the auditor evaluates whether alerts are reviewed, actioned, and tracked to resolution.
✓ Known

Defence in Depth

Layered security approach — multiple independent controls so no single failure creates total compromise.

Defence in depth implements security at multiple layers: physical → network → host → application → data. If the perimeter firewall is breached, network segmentation contains lateral movement. If a server is compromised, encryption protects data at rest. If credentials are stolen, MFA prevents authentication. The auditor evaluates whether critical assets have layered controls — not just perimeter protection with a soft interior (the "M&M" anti-pattern).

🎯 Exam: Defence in depth is the principle behind requiring controls at multiple layers. A flat network with strong perimeter = NOT defence in depth. Any question about compensating controls or layered security maps to this principle.
✓ Known

Digital Signature

Cryptographic mechanism providing authentication, integrity, and non-repudiation for digital content.

Process: hash the message → encrypt hash with sender's private key → attach as signature. Verification: decrypt signature with sender's public key → independently hash message → compare hashes. If they match: message is authentic (only private key holder could sign), unmodified (hash mismatch detects any change), and non-repudiable (signer cannot deny — private key ownership is provable). Used in email signing (S/MIME), code signing, document signing, TLS certificates.

🎯 Exam: Sign with PRIVATE key, verify with PUBLIC key. Encrypt with PUBLIC key, decrypt with PRIVATE key. These are reversed from each other — a very common exam distractor. SHA-256 minimum for signature hashing; SHA-1/MD5 = finding.
✓ Known

Privilege Creep

Gradual accumulation of access rights beyond what is necessary, typically through role changes without access revocation.

Privilege creep occurs when employees change roles and new access is granted without revoking old access. Over time, individuals accumulate permissions far exceeding their current job requirements. Mitigated by: access recertification (periodic manager review), role-based provisioning with automatic revocation on role change, joiner/mover/leaver processes. Privilege creep is the most common access control finding and directly enables insider threat and SoD violations.

🎯 Exam: Any scenario where someone has accumulated access from multiple past roles = privilege creep finding. The remedy is not just removing current excess access — it requires a process fix to prevent recurrence (automated de-provisioning on role change, periodic recertification).
✓ Known

Penetration Testing

Authorised simulation of real-world attacks to identify exploitable vulnerabilities before attackers do.

Penetration testing (pen testing) goes beyond vulnerability scanning — a human tester attempts to actively exploit vulnerabilities, chain them together, and achieve defined objectives (e.g., access to sensitive data, domain admin). Types: Black box (no prior knowledge), White box (full system knowledge), Grey box (partial knowledge — most common). Scope and rules of engagement must be formally documented. Results drive remediation priority. The auditor evaluates pen test scope (comprehensive?), frequency (annual minimum), and whether findings are remediated and verified.

🎯 Exam: Pen testing ≠ vulnerability scanning. Vulnerability scanning identifies potential weaknesses; pen testing validates whether they are actually exploitable and what the business impact would be. Pen test results with no remediation tracking = finding.
✓ Known

Media Sanitisation

Secure destruction or sanitisation of data-bearing media before disposal or reuse to prevent data recovery.

Methods (per NIST SP 800-88): Clear (logical overwrite — sufficient for non-sensitive data reuse), Purge (cryptographic erase or degaussing — for sensitive data, renders recovery infeasible with state-of-the-art techniques), Destroy (physical destruction — shredding, disintegration, incineration — for classified or highly sensitive data). Certificate of destruction required from third-party vendors. SSDs require cryptographic erase (overwrite is insufficient due to wear-levelling). The auditor verifies that a media disposal policy exists and is followed — and that certificates of destruction are retained.

🎯 Exam: Simply deleting files or formatting a drive is NOT secure sanitisation. NIST SP 800-88 is the reference standard. Certificate of destruction from vendor = required for outsourced disposal. SSD overwrite is insufficient — use cryptographic erase.
