About This Domain

What Auditors Must Know

Domain Scope

Domain 4 is the largest CISA domain at 28% — roughly 42 exam questions. It tests operational and resilience thinking across the full IT environment.

  • IT Service Management: ITIL processes, service desk, incident vs. problem management
  • Business Continuity Planning: BIA, BCP development, maintenance and testing
  • Disaster Recovery Planning: DRP strategies, RTO/RPO, recovery sites
  • Database Administration: Access controls, backup/recovery, referential integrity
  • Cloud & Virtualisation: Shared responsibility, cloud models, virtualisation risks
  • Third-Party Risk: Vendor due diligence, SLA monitoring, concentration risk
  • Capacity Management: Performance monitoring, thresholds, capacity planning
  • Network Operations: SIEM, log management, monitoring controls

The Auditor Mindset for Domain 4

Operations auditing is about evaluating whether controls are actually operating — not just documented. Key mindset elements:

  • Test the plan, not just the document: An untested BCP/DRP is the same as no plan. Auditors look for evidence of testing, not just the existence of a document.
  • RTO/RPO are commitments, not aspirations: They must be based on a Business Impact Analysis and validated through actual testing.
  • Incident ≠ Problem: Incident management restores service; problem management finds and eliminates root cause. Both must exist.
  • Cloud does not transfer risk: The organisation remains accountable for controls — only operational responsibility shifts to the provider.
  • Concentration risk: Using one cloud provider, one telecom, or one data centre for everything creates single points of failure that auditors must flag.
  • SLAs must be monitored: A vendor SLA that is never tracked is not a control — it is just a contract clause.
💡

Exam tip: When a question involves a business disruption scenario — always think in order: BIA → BCP → DRP → Test → Maintain. The Business Impact Analysis always comes first.

Section 3

IT Service Management

Incident Management

Goal: restore normal service operation as quickly as possible with minimum business impact. Incident management does NOT investigate root cause.

  • Incident: Any unplanned interruption or quality reduction of an IT service
  • Priority: Determined by Impact × Urgency matrix
  • Workaround: Temporary fix to restore service — not a permanent solution
  • Escalation: Functional (technical skill) or Hierarchical (management awareness)
  • SLA clock: Starts when incident is logged — not when it is assigned
  • Incident record: Must be opened for every incident — no verbal-only resolutions
  • Major Incident: High-impact incident requiring separate process and post-incident review

Problem Management

Goal: identify and eliminate the root cause of incidents to prevent recurrence. Problem management operates independently of incident management.

  • Problem: The unknown underlying cause of one or more incidents
  • Known Error: Problem with identified root cause — workaround may exist even before fix
  • Root Cause Analysis (RCA): Formal investigation — 5 Whys, Fishbone diagram, fault tree analysis
  • KEDB: Known Error Database — enables faster incident resolution using documented workarounds
  • Proactive problem management: Identifies potential problems before they cause incidents
  • Problem record: Separate from incident record — linked but distinct lifecycle
Critical Distinction

Incident management asks "How do we restore service now?"
Problem management asks "Why did this happen and how do we prevent recurrence?"

Service Level Management

SLAs define the committed levels of service between IT and the business. The auditor evaluates whether SLAs exist, are being monitored, and whether breaches are acted upon.

Agreement TypeBetweenPurpose
SLA (Service Level Agreement)IT provider and external customerDefines service levels, penalties, and remedies for a specific service
OLA (Operational Level Agreement)Internal IT teamsInternal agreements supporting delivery of SLA commitments
UC (Underpinning Contract)IT organisation and third-party vendorExternal contracts supporting SLA delivery (e.g., ISP, datacentre)

Key SLA Metrics

  • Availability: 99.9% = 8.7 hrs downtime/year; 99.99% = 52.6 min/year
  • MTTR: Mean Time to Repair — average time to restore service after failure
  • MTBF: Mean Time Between Failures — average time between incidents
  • Response time: Time from incident report to initial response
  • Resolution time: Time from incident report to full resolution
  • Throughput: Volume of transactions processed per unit time

Auditor Questions for SLAs

  • Are SLAs formally documented and signed?
  • Are SLA metrics being actively measured and reported?
  • Are breaches formally tracked and escalated?
  • Are penalty/remedy clauses exercised when warranted?
  • Are SLAs reviewed and updated at least annually?
  • Is there a formal SLA review meeting with the customer?
  • Are OLAs and UCs aligned to support SLA commitments?

Service Desk Controls

The service desk is the single point of contact between users and IT services. Key audit areas:

  • Single point of contact: All service requests and incidents logged — no informal channels
  • Ticketing system: Every interaction creates a record — basis for SLA measurement
  • Categorisation: Consistent categorisation enables trend analysis and problem detection
  • First-call resolution rate: KPI measuring efficiency — higher is better but not at the cost of quality
  • Abandoned call rate: Indicator of understaffing or excessive demand
  • Callback verification: Password resets and sensitive actions must verify caller identity before acting
  • Social engineering risk: Service desks are primary targets for impersonation attacks

Service Desk Audit Findings

Common findings when auditing service desks:

  • Finding: Incidents resolved without creating tickets — no audit trail
  • Finding: Password resets performed without identity verification
  • Finding: No escalation procedures for unresolved incidents
  • Finding: SLA response times not tracked or reported
  • Finding: Privileged actions performed by service desk without authorisation
  • Finding: No user satisfaction measurement or feedback mechanism
  • Finding: Sensitive account changes made via phone without secondary approval

Business Continuity & Disaster Recovery

Expand each step to explore the BCP/DRP development process and audit controls.

Recovery Site Classifications

  • Hot Site: Fully equipped, fully operational, real-time data replication. Can take over within minutes to hours. Most expensive. RTO: <4 hours.
  • Warm Site: Partially equipped, hardware in place but data must be restored from backup. RTO: hours to days. Moderate cost.
  • Cold Site: Facility only — no equipment pre-installed. Organisation ships hardware and restores from backup. RTO: days to weeks. Lowest cost.
  • Mobile Recovery: Portable trailers or units that can be deployed to any location. Useful when primary site is inaccessible.
  • Reciprocal Agreement: Two organisations agree to host each other's operations during a disaster. Risk: capacity constraints when both need it simultaneously.

BCP vs. DRP — Know the Difference

  • BCP (Business Continuity Plan): Broader — covers how the BUSINESS continues to operate during any disruption. Covers people, processes, facilities, communications, and IT.
  • DRP (Disaster Recovery Plan): Narrower — focuses specifically on recovering IT systems and data after a disaster. A subset of BCP.
  • BIA (Business Impact Analysis): Foundation for both. Identifies critical processes, quantifies impact of disruption, and establishes RTO/RPO requirements.
  • Crisis Management Plan: Covers immediate response — evacuation, communications, emergency contacts. Activates before BCP/DRP.
💡

Exam sequence: BIA → BCP → DRP → Test → Maintain. BIA ALWAYS comes first — you cannot determine RTO/RPO without knowing business impact.

Section 6

Database Administration

Database Access Controls

The DBA (Database Administrator) is one of the most privileged roles in IT. Auditors must verify that DBA access is controlled, monitored, and separated from business data access.

  • DBA SoD: DBAs must not also be application developers or business users with data access — they have structural access to all data
  • DBA activity logging: All DBA commands (DDL, DML) must be logged and reviewed — DBAs should not be able to modify their own logs
  • Privileged access monitoring: Real-time alerting on sensitive DBA operations (e.g., DROP TABLE, GRANT privileges)
  • Application service accounts: Applications connect via service accounts — never via personal DBA accounts
  • Least privilege: Users and applications should only have the minimum database permissions required
  • Database auditing: Native database audit logs capture all access — must be retained and protected from DBA tampering

Data Integrity Controls

Referential integrity and data quality controls prevent corrupt or inconsistent data from entering the database.

  • Referential integrity: Foreign key constraints prevent orphaned records — child records cannot exist without a parent
  • Entity integrity: Primary key constraints ensure every row is uniquely identifiable
  • Domain integrity: Data type, format, and range validation — e.g., date field cannot contain text
  • Business rules: Application-level checks enforce business logic — e.g., order total must equal sum of line items
  • Batch reconciliation: Input totals compared to processed totals after every batch run
  • Edit checks: Input validation at point of entry — not only at database level

Backup & Recovery

  • Full backup: Complete copy of all data. Longest backup time, shortest recovery time.
  • Differential backup: All changes since last full backup. Faster than full, slower recovery than incremental.
  • Incremental backup: Only changes since last backup of any type. Fastest backup, slowest recovery (must apply all increments).
  • Transaction log backup: Captures all database transactions — enables point-in-time recovery
  • 3-2-1 Rule: 3 copies of data, 2 different media types, 1 offsite
  • Recovery testing: Backup without tested recovery is not a control — restore tests are mandatory
  • Offsite storage: Backups stored separately from primary site — geographic distance proportionate to risk

DBA Controls — Key Audit Questions

  • Are DBA activities logged and is the log reviewed independently?
  • Can DBAs modify or delete their own audit logs? (If yes — critical finding)
  • Are backup restoration tests performed and documented?
  • When was the last successful full restore test?
  • Are offsite backup copies maintained at an appropriate geographic distance?
  • Are application accounts using least-privilege database permissions?
  • Is there a process to revoke database access when employees leave?
  • Are database patches applied on a defined schedule?

Cloud Computing & Virtualisation

The Shared Responsibility Model

Cloud computing does not transfer accountability — only operational responsibility. The organisation remains accountable to regulators and stakeholders regardless of who operates the control.

Control AreaIaaSPaaSSaaS
Physical infrastructureProviderProviderProvider
Operating systemCustomerProviderProvider
Runtime / middlewareCustomerProviderProvider
Application codeCustomerCustomerProvider
Data classification & protectionCustomerCustomerCustomer
Identity & access managementCustomerCustomerCustomer
Regulatory compliance accountabilityCustomerCustomerCustomer
Critical Principle

Data classification, access management, and regulatory accountability always remain with the customer regardless of deployment model. "The cloud provider handles it" is never an acceptable answer for data protection obligations.

IaaS

Infrastructure as a Service — virtualised compute, storage, and networking.

  • Customer manages OS, middleware, apps, data
  • Maximum flexibility and control
  • Maximum customer security responsibility
  • Examples: AWS EC2, Azure VMs, GCP Compute

PaaS

Platform as a Service — managed runtime and development environment.

  • Customer manages apps and data only
  • Provider manages OS and runtime patches
  • Faster development, less control
  • Examples: Heroku, AWS Elastic Beanstalk

SaaS

Software as a Service — fully managed application delivered via browser.

  • Customer manages only data and access
  • Provider manages everything else
  • Least flexibility, least operational burden
  • Examples: Salesforce, Microsoft 365, Workday

Deployment Models

  • Public Cloud: Shared infrastructure, provider managed, multi-tenant. Lowest cost, highest provider dependency.
  • Private Cloud: Dedicated infrastructure for one organisation. Higher cost, greater control, easier compliance.
  • Hybrid Cloud: Mix of public and private. Sensitive data on private, burst capacity on public.
  • Community Cloud: Shared by organisations with common requirements (e.g., government agencies, healthcare sector).

Cloud Governance Controls

  • Cloud Security Posture Management (CSPM) tools
  • Cloud-native audit logs (CloudTrail, Azure Monitor)
  • Right-to-audit clause in cloud contracts
  • SOC 2 Type II reports from provider (not Type I)
  • Data residency controls and sovereignty requirements
  • Exit strategy and data portability provisions

Cloud-Specific Audit Risks

  • Concentration risk: Single cloud provider failure impacts entire organisation. Multi-cloud strategy mitigates.
  • Data sovereignty: Data may be replicated across jurisdictions — GDPR, HIPAA requirements may be violated without explicit controls.
  • Shadow IT: Business units provisioning cloud services without IT approval — no security review, no contract terms.
  • Lock-in risk: Proprietary formats and services make migration to another provider difficult and expensive.
  • Visibility gap: Cloud provider manages underlying infrastructure — customer cannot independently verify physical security.
  • Misconfiguration: S3 bucket public exposure, open security groups — most cloud breaches are misconfiguration, not provider failure.
  • Shared tenancy: VM escape attacks, noisy neighbour performance issues, cross-tenant data leakage risks.

Auditing Cloud Providers

The auditor cannot directly inspect the cloud provider's physical infrastructure. Instead:

  • SOC 2 Type II report: Independent assessment of provider controls over a period (minimum 6 months). Type II is always preferred over Type I.
  • ISO 27001 certificate: Third-party certified ISMS — verify certificate is current and covers the relevant services
  • CSA STAR: Cloud Security Alliance registry — self-assessment and third-party certification
  • Right-to-audit clause: Must be in contract — may only allow questionnaire-based audit rather than physical inspection
  • Penetration test results: Provider should share relevant pen test reports under NDA

Virtualisation Controls

Virtualisation creates multiple virtual machines (VMs) on shared physical hardware. The hypervisor is the critical control point.

  • Hypervisor security: The hypervisor controls all VMs — compromise of hypervisor = compromise of all VMs on the host
  • VM escape: Attack where a process in a VM breaks out to the hypervisor or other VMs — mitigated by hypervisor patching
  • VM sprawl: Uncontrolled proliferation of VMs — creates unpatched, forgotten systems
  • Snapshot management: VM snapshots capture full state — must be controlled, encrypted, and deleted when no longer needed
  • Network segmentation: Virtual networks must enforce the same segmentation as physical networks

Container & Microservices Risks

  • Container image security: Base images must be scanned for CVEs before deployment — not "pulled from Docker Hub untested"
  • Orchestration security: Kubernetes API server access must be restricted — default settings are often insecure
  • Secrets management: Credentials must not be embedded in container images or environment variables in plaintext
  • Immutable infrastructure: Containers should be replaced, not patched in-place — enables consistent, auditable deployments
  • Service mesh audit: mTLS between services, zero-trust network policies within the cluster

Third-Party Risk Lifecycle

  • Due diligence (pre-contract): Financial stability, security posture, regulatory compliance, references, SOC reports
  • Contract controls: Right-to-audit, SLA, data ownership, breach notification, IP ownership, exit provisions
  • Onboarding: Access provisioning, NDA, security training, integration security review
  • Ongoing monitoring: SLA tracking, periodic reassessment, security questionnaires, incident reporting
  • Offboarding: Access revocation, data return/destruction, key management, contract closure

Concentration Risk

Concentration risk occurs when an organisation depends on a single vendor, provider, or geographic location for critical services — creating a single point of failure.

  • Single cloud provider: Provider outage impacts entire IT estate
  • Single ISP: Connectivity failure isolates the organisation
  • Single data centre: Physical disaster destroys all systems
  • Single COTS vendor: End-of-support leaves organisation exposed
  • Auditor action: Identify all critical single-vendor dependencies and assess whether the organisation has mitigating controls (e.g., multi-cloud, secondary ISP, geographic redundancy)
💡

Exam tip: Concentration risk is always a finding when a critical service has no alternative. The auditor should recommend documented exit strategies and transition plans for all critical vendors.

Capacity Management & Operations Monitoring

Capacity Management Controls

  • Threshold alerting: Automated alerts when resources exceed defined thresholds (e.g., CPU >85%, disk >90%)
  • Capacity planning: Forward-looking analysis — 6–12 month forecasts based on growth projections
  • Performance baselining: Establish normal performance patterns to identify anomalies
  • Trend analysis: Historical data used to predict future capacity needs before thresholds are breached
  • Demand management: Scheduling batch jobs, backups, and intensive tasks during off-peak hours
  • Auto-scaling: Cloud environments should auto-scale to meet demand — auditors verify scaling triggers and limits

SIEM & Log Management

Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) aggregates logs from across the environment to detect security events and support forensic investigation.

  • Log sources: Firewalls, IDS/IPS, servers, applications, databases, identity providers, cloud platforms
  • Log integrity: Logs must be write-protected — systems must not be able to delete their own logs
  • Retention: Typically 12 months online + longer offline archival per regulatory requirements
  • Correlation rules: SIEM detects patterns across multiple log sources (e.g., failed login + privilege escalation)
  • Alert tuning: Too many false positives = alert fatigue = missed real incidents
  • SOC coverage: 24/7 monitoring for critical systems — alerts with no one watching are not controls
  • Auditor check: SIEM exists but are alerts reviewed? Are they actioned? Are response times within SLA?

Real-World Scenarios

Choose the correct auditor response, then read the expert explanation.

Scenario 1 — Business Continuity

The Untested BCP

During an audit, you discover that the organisation has a comprehensive, 200-page Business Continuity Plan last updated 18 months ago. When asked when the plan was last tested, the IT Director says "we ran a tabletop exercise two years ago." The organisation processes online payments 24/7 with an RTO of 2 hours documented in the plan.

What is the auditor's MOST important finding?

An untested BCP is the same as no BCP. Tabletop exercises have value but do not validate whether systems can actually recover within the RTO. For a 24/7 payment processor with a 2-hour RTO, the auditor must find: (1) No failover test has been conducted — the 2-hour RTO has never been validated against actual recovery capability; (2) The plan is 18 months out of date — systems, personnel, and processes have likely changed; (3) BCP testing frequency is insufficient — payment processors should test at minimum annually, ideally semi-annually. The auditor should recommend an immediate full failover test (during a planned maintenance window) to validate the RTO, and establish a formal testing schedule with documented results.

Scenario 2 — Cloud Risk

The Cloud Misconception

A CISO tells you: "We moved our customer database to AWS last year, so AWS is responsible for data security and GDPR compliance now. We reviewed their SOC 2 Type I report and they passed, so we're satisfied with their security posture." The database contains PII of 500,000 EU customers.

How many distinct findings does this statement contain?

This statement contains three distinct findings: (1) Cloud does not transfer GDPR accountability. Under GDPR, the customer organisation is the Data Controller — they remain fully accountable to regulators regardless of who operates the infrastructure. AWS is a Data Processor — responsible for operating controls, but the controller remains liable. (2) Data security responsibility does not transfer. IAM configuration, data classification, encryption key management, and access controls remain the customer's responsibility under the shared responsibility model. (3) SOC 2 Type I is insufficient. Type I is a point-in-time assessment of control design. Type II evaluates operating effectiveness over a minimum 6-month period. For ongoing assurance, only Type II is appropriate. The auditor should also check whether a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) was executed with AWS — required under GDPR Article 28.

Scenario 3 — Third-Party Risk

The Critical Single Vendor

Your organisation's entire payroll processing — covering 15,000 employees — is outsourced to a single SaaS vendor. During audit, you discover: no right-to-audit clause in the contract, no SLA for processing downtime, the vendor has not provided a SOC 2 report, and there is no documented exit strategy if the vendor ceases operations. Payroll must process by the 25th of each month or employees are not paid on time.

What is the FIRST action the auditor should recommend?

The combination of factors here represents a critical, enterprise-level risk that requires immediate escalation — not a recommendation to address at contract renewal. The auditor should: (1) Escalate immediately to the Board/Audit Committee — 15,000 employees not being paid on time creates regulatory exposure (employment law), reputational damage, and potential industrial action. (2) Issue a critical finding covering: no right-to-audit, no SLA, no SOC 2, no exit strategy, and concentration risk. (3) Recommend emergency remediation: Negotiate contractual protections immediately (not at renewal), obtain the SOC 2 report, develop an exit strategy, and document manual payroll contingency procedures. Recommending immediate termination would be inappropriate — the auditor's role is to report risk and recommend remediation, not to make operational decisions. The SOC 2 is one piece of evidence but does not address the more urgent contract gaps.

✓ Known

RTO

Recovery Time Objective — maximum acceptable time to restore a service after disruption.

RTO is determined by the Business Impact Analysis based on how long the business can survive without the service. It drives the choice of recovery strategy: a 2-hour RTO requires a hot site; a 72-hour RTO may allow a cold site. RTO must always be less than MTD (Maximum Tolerable Downtime).

🎯 Exam: RTO is set by the BUSINESS through BIA — not by IT. Auditors verify that the chosen recovery strategy can actually achieve the stated RTO through testing.
✓ Known

RPO

Recovery Point Objective — maximum acceptable data loss measured in time.

RPO determines how frequently backups or replication must occur. An RPO of 1 hour means backups must run at least every hour. An RPO of 0 (zero data loss) requires synchronous real-time replication. RPO is also set by the business through BIA — representing how much data loss the business can tolerate before operations are critically impacted.

🎯 Exam: RPO drives backup frequency. If RPO = 4 hours, backups must run every 4 hours maximum. If daily backup exists but RPO is 4 hours — that is a gap.
✓ Known

BIA

Business Impact Analysis — the foundation of all continuity planning, quantifying the impact of disruption.

The BIA identifies critical business processes, quantifies the financial and operational impact of their disruption over time, establishes MTD/RTO/RPO requirements, and identifies dependencies (systems, people, facilities). The BIA output drives all BCP/DRP decisions. Without a current BIA, RTO/RPO figures are guesses, not commitments.

🎯 Exam: BIA ALWAYS comes before BCP/DRP development. Any question involving "what should happen first" in continuity planning — the answer is BIA.
✓ Known

Hot / Warm / Cold Site

Recovery site classifications based on readiness level and cost.

Hot site: fully equipped and operational, real-time data sync, takeover in minutes — highest cost. Warm site: hardware ready, data restored from backup — hours to days, moderate cost. Cold site: facility only, equipment shipped in and configured — days to weeks, lowest cost. Site choice must match the RTO commitment from the BIA.

🎯 Exam: Match site type to RTO. 2-hour RTO = hot site. 24-hour RTO = warm site. 72+ hour RTO = cold site may be acceptable. A cold site with a 4-hour RTO is a contradiction and a finding.
✓ Known

Incident vs. Problem

Incident restores service; Problem eliminates root cause.

Incident management: restore service fast, minimise impact — workarounds are acceptable. Problem management: identify why the incident happened and permanently eliminate the root cause. A Known Error is a problem with identified root cause — it may have a workaround before a permanent fix is deployed. KEDB (Known Error Database) enables faster incident resolution using documented workarounds.

🎯 Exam: "Server rebooted to restore service" = incident management. "Investigation found memory leak in application version 3.2, patch deployed" = problem management. Both must exist and be separate processes.
✓ Known

SLA / OLA / UC

Three tiers of service level agreements supporting IT service delivery.

SLA (Service Level Agreement): between IT provider and external customer — defines service commitments. OLA (Operational Level Agreement): between internal IT teams — supports SLA delivery. UC (Underpinning Contract): between IT and external supplier — ensures third-party commitments support SLA. All three must be aligned — an SLA promising 99.9% availability is unachievable if the UC with the data centre only guarantees 99%.

🎯 Exam: SLA = external commitment. OLA = internal support agreement. UC = vendor contract supporting delivery. The auditor checks that all three are aligned.
✓ Known

Shared Responsibility Model

Framework defining which security controls are owned by the cloud provider vs. the customer.

In IaaS: provider owns physical/network/hypervisor; customer owns OS, middleware, apps, data, IAM. In SaaS: provider owns almost everything; customer still owns data classification, user access management, and regulatory accountability. Key principle: moving to cloud does not transfer compliance obligations — the data controller is always accountable under GDPR regardless of who operates the platform.

🎯 Exam: Data classification and IAM ALWAYS remain with the customer in every cloud model. GDPR compliance accountability ALWAYS remains with the data controller — not the cloud provider.
✓ Known

Concentration Risk

Excessive dependency on a single vendor, provider, or location — creating a single point of failure.

Concentration risk applies to: single cloud provider, single ISP, single data centre location, single COTS vendor for a critical system. The auditor identifies concentration risk and assesses whether mitigating controls exist (e.g., multi-cloud strategy, secondary ISP, geographic redundancy, documented exit strategy). Concentration risk is always a finding when a critical process has no alternative path.

🎯 Exam: Any scenario where a single vendor failure would stop critical operations = concentration risk finding. The remediation is always diversification OR a documented, tested failover to an alternative.
✓ Known

SIEM

Security Information and Event Management — centralised log aggregation and correlation for security monitoring.

SIEM collects logs from firewalls, servers, applications, identity systems, and cloud platforms. It correlates events across sources to detect patterns (e.g., failed logins followed by privilege escalation from the same IP). Key audit questions: Are alerts reviewed? Are response times within SLA? Are logs protected from tampering? Is there 24/7 SOC coverage? A SIEM with no one watching alerts is not a security control.

🎯 Exam: SIEM exists = control design. SIEM alerts reviewed and actioned within SLA = control effectiveness. Only operating effectiveness matters for audit conclusions.
✓ Known

MTTR / MTBF

Key reliability metrics: Mean Time to Repair and Mean Time Between Failures.

MTTR: average time to restore service after a failure — measures recovery efficiency. Lower is better. MTBF: average time between failures — measures reliability. Higher is better. Together they determine system availability: Availability = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR). These are used in SLAs and capacity planning to assess whether systems meet availability commitments.

🎯 Exam: MTTR measures how fast you recover. MTBF measures how reliable the system is. Both feed into availability calculations and SLA compliance reporting.
✓ Known

SOC 2 Type II

Independent audit report assessing a service provider's controls over a defined operating period.

SOC 2 reports assess controls across five Trust Service Criteria: Security, Availability, Processing Integrity, Confidentiality, and Privacy. Type I: point-in-time assessment of control design only. Type II: assesses operating effectiveness over a period (minimum 6 months) — always preferred for ongoing vendor assurance. The auditor should verify the report covers the relevant trust service criteria and is current (within 12 months).

🎯 Exam: SOC 2 Type I = control design assessed (point in time). SOC 2 Type II = controls operating effectively over time. For vendor assurance — always Type II. Type I alone is insufficient for ongoing assurance.
✓ Known

3-2-1 Backup Rule

Best-practice backup strategy: 3 copies, 2 media types, 1 offsite.

3 copies of data total (production + 2 backups); 2 different storage media (e.g., disk and tape, or disk and cloud); 1 copy stored offsite at an appropriate geographic distance from the primary site. This rule protects against hardware failure (3 copies), media failure (2 types), and site-level disaster (1 offsite). The auditor verifies that restore tests are performed — backup without tested recovery is not a control.

🎯 Exam: The 3-2-1 rule is the backup standard. A backup strategy with all copies onsite fails the "1 offsite" requirement and is a finding for any system with a DRP requirement.
✓ Known

VM Sprawl

Uncontrolled proliferation of virtual machines that are forgotten, unpatched, and unmonitored.

VM sprawl occurs when virtual machines are easy to create but there is no lifecycle management process to decommission them. Forgotten VMs accumulate unpatched vulnerabilities, may retain sensitive data, and create undocumented attack surfaces. The auditor should check: Is there an inventory of all VMs? Are VMs patched on the same schedule as physical servers? Is there a decommissioning process? Are orphaned VMs identified and removed?

🎯 Exam: VM sprawl = lack of asset inventory control. Unpatched VMs = vulnerability management finding. The same controls that apply to physical servers must apply to VMs.
✓ Known

Referential Integrity

Database constraint ensuring that foreign key relationships between tables remain consistent.

Referential integrity prevents orphaned records — a child record (e.g., order line item) cannot exist without a valid parent record (e.g., order header). Enforced through foreign key constraints at the database level. Without referential integrity, data quality degrades over time through orphaned records, incorrect joins, and corrupted reporting. The auditor should verify referential integrity is enforced at the database level — not just in application code, which can be bypassed.

🎯 Exam: Referential integrity enforced at database level = preventive control. Application-only validation = weaker control (can be bypassed with direct database access). Database-level enforcement is always stronger.
