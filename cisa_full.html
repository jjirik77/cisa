About the Certification

What is the CISA?

The CISA Credential

The Certified Information Systems Auditor (CISA) is issued by ISACA — the global authority on IT governance, audit, and security since 1969. It is the world's most widely held IS audit certification.

  • Global recognition: Held by over 170,000 professionals in 180+ countries
  • Industry standard: Required or preferred by Big Four firms, financial regulators, governments, and Fortune 500 audit departments worldwide
  • Career impact: CISA holders command 20–30% salary premiums over non-certified peers in most markets
  • Employer demand: CISA consistently ranks in the top 5 most in-demand cybersecurity and audit certifications globally
  • ANSI accredited: Accredited under ISO/IEC 17024 — meaning it meets the highest international standard for personnel certification

Who Should Pursue CISA?

CISA is designed for professionals who audit, control, monitor, and assess IT and business systems. Ideal candidates include:

  • IS/IT Auditors: Internal and external auditors assessing technology controls
  • Compliance Officers: Professionals evaluating regulatory compliance (SOX, GDPR, HIPAA, PCI-DSS)
  • IT Risk Managers: Practitioners assessing and managing IT-related risks
  • Security Professionals: Those with security responsibilities who want formal audit credentials
  • IT Consultants: Advisors on IT governance, controls, and risk frameworks
  • Public Accountants: CPAs expanding into IT audit and systems assurance
💡

Career tip: Many organisations now list CISA as a requirement (not just preference) for senior IS audit roles. Earning it before seeking promotion significantly increases negotiating leverage.

Eligibility & Cost

Requirements & Fees

Eligibility Requirements

You can sit the exam at any time — but to use the CISA designation, you must meet these requirements within 5 years of passing:

  • 5 years total of professional work experience in IS audit, control, assurance, or security
  • 3 of those 5 years must be in IS audit, control, or security — directly performing or supervising IS audit work
  • Substitutions allowed: University degrees can substitute up to 2 years (bachelor's) or 1 year (60 credit hours / associate degree) of the general experience requirement — NOT the 3-year IS audit core
  • CISM, CGEIT, or CRISC: Holding another ISACA credential can substitute 1 year of experience
  • Verification: Employers must verify work experience — forms submitted through the ISACA online portal
  • Code of ethics: Must agree to adhere to ISACA's Code of Professional Ethics
Important

You may sit the exam and receive a score before meeting all experience requirements. You have 5 years from the exam date to apply for the full certification with verified experience.

Keeping Your Credential

CPE & Certification Maintenance

Continuing Professional Education (CPE)

To maintain the CISA designation, you must earn and report CPE hours annually. CPE demonstrates that your knowledge remains current.

  • Annual requirement: Minimum 20 CPE hours per year
  • 3-year requirement: Minimum 120 CPE hours over any 3-year rolling period
  • Reporting: CPE hours reported via the ISACA online portal — not self-certified; subject to audit
  • Qualifying activities: Training courses, conferences, webinars, university courses, writing articles, speaking, volunteer chapter work, self-study with verification
  • ISACA events: ISACA conferences, webinars, and chapter events typically provide CPE hours directly
  • Deadline: December 31 of each calendar year — late reporting triggers a reinstatement fee

Maintaining Active Status

  • Annual maintenance fee: Paid every year regardless of CPE status — required to keep the designation active
  • CPE audit: ISACA may randomly audit your CPE records — retain documentation for all claimed activities
  • Lapsed certification: If CPE or fees lapse, the CISA becomes inactive. Reinstatement requires a fee and catching up on CPE — the exam does not need to be retaken
  • Ethics compliance: Any professional ethics violations must be disclosed — ISACA investigates and may revoke the credential
  • Exam content updates: CISA content is reviewed every 3–5 years. Major updates are communicated in advance — no re-certification exam required for existing holders
💡

CPE tip: Reading industry publications, completing online courses (LinkedIn Learning, Coursera, SANS), and attending local ISACA chapter meetings all count as CPE hours. Document everything as you go — retroactive documentation is difficult.

Identify your weakest domain from practice test performance and dedicate final study time there. Re-review glossaries for domains where you're scoring below 70%. Focus on understanding concepts, not memorising answers.

Review key terms, not new material. Confirm your exam appointment, travel route, and ID requirements. Sleep well the night before. Arrive 30 minutes early for in-person exams. For online proctored exams — test your tech setup 24 hours before.

Audit Planning & Risk Assessment

Audit Charter

The audit charter is the formal document authorising the internal audit function. Without a charter, the audit has no mandate.

  • Purpose & objectives: Defines why the audit function exists
  • Authority: Grants right of access to people, records, systems, and property
  • Responsibility: Defines scope — what IS and IS NOT in scope
  • Independence: Auditor must be independent from auditee — structural and functional
  • Approved by: Audit committee or board — not management (who are the auditees)
  • Reviewed annually: Charter kept current; scope changes require formal amendment

Audit Planning Process

  • Audit universe: Complete inventory of all auditable entities — systems, processes, locations, functions
  • Risk-based planning: High-risk areas audited more frequently — risk appetite drives audit frequency
  • Audit program: Detailed plan specifying objectives, scope, timing, resources, and procedures
  • Preliminary survey: Background research before fieldwork — org structure, prior audit findings, regulatory landscape
  • Resource planning: Assign auditors with appropriate skills; address conflicts of interest
  • Engagement letter: Formal notification to auditee of upcoming audit — scope, timing, contact
💡

Exam tip: The audit committee authorises the audit charter — not management. Independence is structural (reporting line) AND functional (freedom from influence).

Audit Risk Model

AR = IR × CR × DR

  • Inherent Risk (IR): Risk before any controls — the natural risk of the area being audited. Auditor CANNOT control IR.
  • Control Risk (CR): Risk that existing controls fail to prevent/detect material misstatements. Auditor CANNOT control CR.
  • Detection Risk (DR): Risk that auditor's procedures fail to detect remaining errors. Auditor CAN control DR — by adjusting sample size and testing rigor.
  • Key insight: When IR and CR are high, the auditor must lower DR by doing MORE testing
The Rule

Detection Risk is the ONLY component the auditor controls. IR and CR are assessed, not controlled.

Materiality

  • Quantitative: Dollar thresholds — errors exceeding a defined amount are material (e.g., >5% of net income)
  • Qualitative: Nature-based — some items are material regardless of size (e.g., fraud, regulatory violations, related-party transactions)
  • Performance materiality: Set below overall materiality — provides buffer for multiple small errors that collectively become material
  • Tolerable error rate: Maximum deviation rate the auditor will accept from expected controls performance
  • Risk matrix: Likelihood × Impact grid used to prioritise audit areas — high-high areas receive most audit attention

Key Standards

  • ISACA IS Audit Standards: The authoritative standard for CISA practitioners — auditors must comply
  • COBIT 2019: Primary IT governance framework — 5 domains, 40 objectives, capability levels 0–5
  • ISO 27001: ISMS framework — information security management system
  • COSO ERM: Enterprise risk management framework widely used in SOX compliance context
  • ITIL v4: IT service management best practices — referenced in operations audits
  • NIST CSF 2.0: Cybersecurity framework with Identify/Protect/Detect/Respond/Recover functions

Audit Evidence

Evidence reliability hierarchy (most to least reliable):

  • 1st — Re-performance: Auditor redoes the process independently — most reliable
  • 2nd — External confirmation: Evidence from third parties external to the organisation
  • 3rd — Observation: Auditor directly observes process or physical asset
  • 4th — Internal documentation: Organisation's own documents — lower reliability, can be altered
  • 5th — Inquiry: Verbal or written statements from management/staff — least reliable alone
💡

CAATs: Computer-Assisted Audit Techniques — tools enabling auditors to test 100% of transactions rather than samples. Types: GAS, Test Data, Parallel Simulation, EAM, ITF.

Practice Scenarios

Scenario 1 — Audit Independence

The Conflicted Auditor

An IS auditor is asked to audit the new ERP system her department helped implement last year. She designed several key control specifications and trained the end users.

What should the auditor do?

Independence is a foundational auditing principle. An auditor cannot objectively evaluate work she participated in creating — this creates a self-review threat. ISACA standards require auditors to disclose any real or perceived impairments to independence to audit management. The appropriate action is full recusal: a different auditor with no involvement in the ERP implementation should conduct the audit. Disclosing in the report alone is insufficient — the conflict must be escalated before any work begins.

Scenario 2 — Audit Risk

The High-Risk Assessment

During planning, you assess that the accounts payable process has High Inherent Risk and that existing preventive controls have significant gaps (High Control Risk). Management asks you to limit your testing to 5 transactions to "keep the audit efficient."

What is the correct auditor response?

When IR × CR = high, the audit risk model requires the auditor to achieve low Detection Risk through extensive substantive testing. Management cannot dictate the auditor's sample size — this is a scope restriction that impairs the auditor's ability to gather sufficient appropriate evidence. If management persists, the auditor should escalate to the audit committee and consider whether a scope qualification is needed. Working papers must document the restriction and the auditor's response. Five transactions for a high-risk, high-volume process is not sufficient evidence basis for any conclusion.

IT Governance & Frameworks

Governance vs. Management

The most tested distinction in Domain 2. Governance sets direction; management executes.

  • Governance (EDM): Evaluate, Direct, Monitor — performed by the Board and senior executives. Answers: "Are we doing the right things?"
  • Management (PBRM): Plan, Build, Run, Monitor — performed by management and IT staff. Answers: "Are we doing things right?"
  • Board responsibility: Setting risk appetite, approving IT strategy, overseeing IT investment, ensuring compliance
  • CIO responsibility: IT strategy execution, resource management, service delivery — NOT governance
  • Audit committee: Oversight of financial reporting and internal audit — reports to board, not management
💡

Exam tip: Any question asking who is ULTIMATELY RESPONSIBLE for IT governance — the answer is the Board/Senior Management, not the CISO or CIO.

Key Performance Indicators

  • KGI (Key Goal Indicator): Measures outcomes — lag indicators. Tells you whether you achieved the goal AFTER the fact. E.g., "% of IT projects delivered on time."
  • KPI (Key Performance Indicator): Measures activity — lead indicators. Tracks progress toward goals IN REAL TIME. E.g., "% of milestones completed per sprint."
  • KRI (Key Risk Indicator): Early warning signals that risk is increasing. E.g., "% of unpatched critical systems." Triggers action before a loss occurs.
  • BSC (Balanced Scorecard): 4-perspective strategy framework: Financial, Customer, Internal Process, Learning & Growth

Enterprise Risk Management

  • Risk appetite: Amount of risk the board is willing to accept — set at governance level, expressed in policy
  • Risk tolerance: Operational variance around risk appetite — how far management can deviate before escalating
  • ALE formula: ALE = SLE × ARO (Annual Loss Expectancy = Single Loss Expectancy × Annualised Rate of Occurrence)
  • Risk response options: Accept (tolerate within appetite), Avoid (eliminate the activity), Transfer (insurance/outsource), Mitigate (implement controls)
  • Residual risk: Risk remaining after controls applied — must be within risk appetite
  • Risk register: Documented inventory of identified risks with ratings, owners, and treatment plans

Privacy & Compliance Overview

  • GDPR: 72-hour breach notification, €20M/4% penalty, 6 lawful bases, DPO requirement for high-risk processing, Article 25 Privacy by Design
  • CCPA/CPRA: California opt-out model, $7,500 per intentional violation, right to know/delete/portability
  • HIPAA: 60-day breach notification, Privacy Officer + Security Officer required, Business Associate Agreements
  • Data classification: Public → Internal → Confidential → Restricted (most sensitive)
  • Policy hierarchy: Policy → Standard → Procedure → Guideline

Change Management Categories

  • Standard change: Pre-approved, routine, low risk. No individual CAB approval needed. E.g., monthly patch application following approved schedule.
  • Normal change: Requires CAB review and approval. Standard process — risk assessment, business impact, rollback plan, test results required before approval.
  • Emergency change: Critical fix required outside normal process. Expedited CAB (e-CAB) or verbal approval. MANDATORY PIR after the change. SoD must still be maintained.
💡

Exam tip: Emergency changes still require approval (even if expedited) and ALWAYS require a post-implementation review. "Emergency" never justifies skipping approval — it only justifies expedited approval.

Configuration Management

  • CMDB (Configuration Management Database): Authoritative record of all IT assets and their relationships — must be updated at change closure
  • Baseline configuration: Approved, documented starting configuration — deviations require formal change
  • Configuration audit: Verifies actual configuration matches CMDB baseline
  • Version control: All code tracked in version control system — no manual edits outside VCS
  • SoD in change: Developer must not deploy to production. Code reviewer must be different from code author.

IT Service Management & BCP/DRP

Incident vs. Problem Management

  • Incident: Unplanned interruption — objective is to RESTORE SERVICE as quickly as possible. Workarounds are acceptable.
  • Problem: Root cause investigation — objective is to ELIMINATE ROOT CAUSE and prevent recurrence. Requires structured RCA.
  • KEDB (Known Error Database): Documented known problems with approved workarounds — enables faster incident resolution
  • MTTR (Mean Time to Repair/Restore): Average time from incident detection to service restoration
  • MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures): Average time between service failures — higher is better
💡

Exam tip: Incident = restore quickly. Problem = fix permanently. An incident that recurs without root cause investigation is a problem management failure.

SLA / OLA / UC Hierarchy

  • SLA (Service Level Agreement): Between IT and the business/customers — defines service standards from the customer's perspective
  • OLA (Operational Level Agreement): Between internal IT teams — supports the SLA commitments (e.g., between Service Desk and Network team)
  • UC (Underpinning Contract): Between IT and external suppliers — underpins both SLA and OLA commitments
  • Hierarchy: UC supports OLA which supports SLA. If the UC fails, the SLA fails — third-party contracts must be monitored
  • Social engineering risk: Service desk staff social engineering vulnerability — verify caller identity BEFORE resetting passwords or granting access

Cloud Shared Responsibility

  • IaaS: Customer manages OS, applications, data. Provider manages hypervisor, hardware, physical security.
  • PaaS: Customer manages applications and data. Provider manages everything below the application layer.
  • SaaS: Customer manages data and user access configuration. Provider manages everything else.
  • Always customer's responsibility regardless of cloud model: data classification, IAM/access controls, and GDPR accountability — these never transfer to the cloud provider
  • SOC 2 Type I: Point-in-time design effectiveness only. SOC 2 Type II: Operating effectiveness over a period — always preferred for vendor assurance

Third-Party Risk

  • Concentration risk: Dependence on a single vendor/provider/geography — critical finding if organisation has no alternative
  • Vendor due diligence: Before engagement — financial stability, security posture, geographic risk, regulatory compliance
  • Ongoing monitoring: Annual SOC 2 Type II review, security questionnaires, penetration test results, regulatory alerts
  • Right-to-audit clause: Contract must grant the right to audit the vendor or their SOC reports — without this, oversight is impossible
  • Exit strategy: Data return and portability provisions — what happens to your data if the vendor fails or the contract ends?

Access, Cryptography & Network Security

IAM Controls

  • Joiner/Mover/Leaver: Access provisioned on day one per role, re-provisioned (old access revoked before new granted) on role change, disabled immediately on termination
  • Privilege creep: Accumulated excess access from role changes — most common IAM finding. Fixed by access recertification and automated de-provisioning.
  • Orphan accounts: Active accounts with no associated active employee — critical finding
  • Access recertification: Managers periodically certify each user's access is still appropriate — rubber-stamp approvals with no actual review = ineffective control
  • SoD enforcement: IAM must prevent conflicting role assignments — e.g., cannot be both payment initiator and payment approver

PAM & Authorisation Models

  • JIT (Just-in-Time) access: Privileged access granted for specific task window only — no standing admin accounts
  • Session recording: All privileged sessions recorded — PAM logs must not be modifiable by admins themselves
  • RBAC: Role-based — most common enterprise model; privilege creep risk
  • MAC: Mandatory (government/military) — system-enforced labels, cannot be overridden by user
  • ABAC: Attribute-based — most flexible and fine-grained; complex to audit
  • MFA rule: Two different factor TYPES required — two passwords ≠ MFA. FIDO2 hardware keys are phishing-resistant (strongest).

Key management rule: Encryption keys must NEVER be stored in the same location as the data they encrypt. Keys belong in an HSM or dedicated KMS, not a database column next to encrypted records.

Network Security

  • DMZ: Perimeter network for public-facing services — compromise does not automatically grant internal access
  • Flat network: No internal segmentation — critical finding; one compromised device threatens all devices
  • IDS: Detects and alerts (passive, out-of-band). IPS: Detects and blocks (active, inline).
  • WAF: Web Application Firewall — inspects HTTP/HTTPS payload; required for web applications; detects OWASP Top 10
  • Implicit deny: All traffic blocked unless explicitly permitted — "permit all, deny specific" is a finding

Zero Trust Principles

  • Never trust, always verify: Network location does not grant implicit trust — every request authenticated and authorised regardless of origin
  • Verify explicitly: Continuous authentication — identity, device health, request context evaluated on every access
  • Least privilege: Minimum access for the specific task; JIT for elevated access
  • Assume breach: Design for internal attacker — micro-segmentation, encrypt internal traffic, monitor east-west traffic
  • Auditor note: Zero Trust is a philosophy, not a product — evaluate actual controls (MFA, segmentation, monitoring), not vendor claims

Domain 5 — Section 4

Practice Scenarios

Scenario 1 — Access Controls

Privilege Creep + SoD Violation

The Finance Director has accumulated roles from three previous positions. She can now create suppliers, initiate payments, and approve those same payments — all in the same financial system.

What is the auditor's PRIMARY finding?

This is a critical dual finding. Privilege creep: accumulated permissions from prior roles never revoked during role transitions — the most common access control finding in enterprise environments. SoD violation: create supplier + initiate payment + approve payment in the same system = the classic fraud-enabling combination. One person can create a fictitious vendor, submit fraudulent invoices, and self-approve payments with zero other involvement. MFA does not compensate for SoD violations — it authenticates identity, but does not prevent abuse of legitimately authenticated sessions. Remedy: immediate revocation of legacy permissions, SoD matrix defining conflicting role combinations, and quarterly access recertification with manager certification of each user's access.

Scenario 2 — Breach Notification

The Exposed Database

A 50,000-record EU customer PII database has been found publicly accessible with no authentication for an unknown period. It is now Day 2 since discovery. The IT manager says "We don't have confirmed evidence of access, so no notification required yet."

What should the auditor advise?

GDPR Article 33 requires supervisory authority notification within 72 hours of becoming aware of a personal data breach — the clock started at discovery, making this Day 2 of a 72-hour window. A personal data breach under GDPR includes any incident leading to unauthorised disclosure of or access to personal data — an unauthenticated public database qualifies as an unauthorised disclosure risk even without proven exfiltration. The unknown exposure period prevents the organisation from ruling out access, which typically makes notification mandatory. The auditor should immediately escalate to the DPO and legal counsel. Waiting for complete forensic certainty before notifying has been the basis for significant GDPR enforcement actions.

Exam Strategy

15 Tips for Exam Day Success

01

Think Like an Auditor

Every question is from an auditor's perspective — not a security engineer or IT manager. Ask: "What would an auditor assess, verify, or recommend?" The auditor validates controls; they don't implement them.

02

Read All Four Options

Never stop at the first answer that seems correct. CISA questions often have two plausible-sounding options — the correct one is the BEST answer, not just a correct one. Read all four before choosing.

03

Eliminate Clearly Wrong Answers

Narrow from four to two using elimination. Look for absolutes ("never," "always") as warning signs, and eliminate answers that describe non-auditor actions (implementing controls, managing people, etc.).

04

FIRST / BEST / MOST Qualifiers

When the question asks what auditor should do "FIRST" or "MOST importantly" — the order matters. Planning before testing. BIA before BCP. Independence before fieldwork. These sequences are testable.

05

Time Management — 96 Seconds

You have ~96 seconds per question. Flag difficult questions and return — don't get stuck. If you're genuinely unsure after 2 minutes, make your best guess, flag it, and move on. Return at the end.

06

Domain 4 Is 28% — Prioritise It

Nearly one-third of exam questions come from Domain 4. If time is limited in your final review, Domain 4 has the highest ROI. BCP/DRP, BIA, RTO/RPO, and ITSM are the most frequently tested topics.

07

No Negative Marking

There is no penalty for wrong answers. Never leave a question blank — always guess. A blank question is guaranteed zero; a guess has a 25% chance of being correct.

08

450 = Pass (Not 75%)

The CISA uses a scaled scoring model (200–800). Passing is 450 — not a percentage. Don't convert your practice scores to percentages and assume the same threshold applies on the actual exam.

09

Board is Always Accountable

When asked who is ULTIMATELY responsible for IT governance, data protection, or risk — the answer is always the Board or Senior Management, not the CISO, CIO, or IT team. Governance belongs at the top.

10

Process Before Technology

CISA questions often have both a process-based and technology-based answer. The process answer is usually correct. Implement a policy, conduct an assessment, or perform a review — before buying a tool.

11

Independence is Sacred

Any answer that compromises auditor independence is wrong. If an auditor helped implement a system, they cannot audit it. If management sets the sample size, that's a scope restriction. Independence before everything.

12

BIA Always Comes First

Any question about business continuity, disaster recovery, or crisis management — if the options include conducting a BIA, that is always the first step. Always. You cannot define recovery objectives without knowing business impact.

13

Untested = Uncontrolled

A backup that has never been restored is not a control. A DRP that has never been tested is not a control. A SIEM with no one monitoring alerts is not a control. Testing and monitoring make controls effective.

14

Watch for Reasonable Answers

CISA exam writers are skilled at creating distractors that sound authoritative. If an answer seems extreme ("immediately shut down all systems") or implies the auditor takes on management responsibility, it's likely wrong.

15

Review Your Flags, Then Submit

Use the last 10–15 minutes to review flagged questions. Don't change answers unless you have a specific, concrete reason — initial instincts based on solid preparation are often correct. Trust your preparation.
