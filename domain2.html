COBIT 2019 Deep Dive

COBIT 2019 replaced COBIT 5 with a more flexible, customizable approach using design factors to tailor governance system to organizational context. It features 40 governance and management objectives across 5 domains.

Auditor Use of COBIT

IS auditors use COBIT capability levels (0–5: Incomplete → Optimizing) to rate IT process maturity, identify gaps against target performance, and prioritize audit recommendations. COBIT is the primary reference cited in ISACA exam questions on IT governance.

COBIT 2019 Design Factors

  • Enterprise strategy (growth, compliance, innovation)
  • Enterprise goals and IT-related goals cascade
  • Risk profile of the organization
  • Current IT-related issues and pain points
  • Threat landscape (industry/geography)
  • Compliance requirements (regulatory, contractual)
  • Role of IT (support, factory, turnaround, strategic)
  • IT sourcing model (insourced, outsourced, hybrid)
  • IT implementation methods (agile, DevOps, waterfall)

Governance System Components

  • Processes: Structured governance/management activities
  • Organizational structures: Committees, roles, accountabilities
  • Principles, policies, frameworks: Behavioral guidance
  • Information: Data for governance decisions
  • Culture, ethics, behavior: Tone at the top
  • People, skills, competencies: Human capital
  • Services, infrastructure, applications: Technology enablers

ITIL v4 — IT Service Management

ITIL (Information Technology Infrastructure Library) v4 is the most widely adopted IT service management framework. It focuses on the co-creation of value between IT and business stakeholders.

ITIL vs COBIT

COBIT covers IT governance broadly (strategy → control → assurance). ITIL focuses specifically on IT service delivery and lifecycle management. An IS auditor uses COBIT to assess governance; uses ITIL as the standard against which service management processes are evaluated.

ITIL 4 Service Value Chain

  • Plan: Ensure shared direction for the organization
  • Improve: Continual improvement across all areas
  • Engage: Understand stakeholder needs
  • Design & Transition: Build and deploy services
  • Obtain/Build: Acquire service components
  • Deliver & Support: Deliver agreed services

ITIL 4 Guiding Principles

  • Focus on value
  • Start where you are
  • Progress iteratively with feedback
  • Collaborate and promote visibility
  • Think and work holistically
  • Keep it simple and practical
  • Optimize and automate

ISO/IEC 38500 — IT Governance

  • International standard for corporate governance of IT
  • Six principles: Responsibility, Strategy, Acquisition, Performance, Conformance, Human Behaviour
  • Directed at board and senior executives
  • Covers evaluation, direction, and monitoring (EDM model)
  • Auditors assess whether the board exercises adequate IT governance per 38500 principles

ISO/IEC 27001 — ISMS

  • Specifies requirements for an Information Security Management System
  • Risk-based approach to managing information security
  • Annex A: 93 security controls across 4 themes
  • Certification requires third-party audit
  • Auditors assess ISMS design and operating effectiveness against ISO 27001 requirements

ISO 31000 — Risk Management

  • International standard for enterprise risk management principles
  • Provides principles, framework, and process for any type of risk
  • Not sector-specific — applies to all organizations
  • Key principle: Risk management is integral to governance
  • Auditors verify ERM programs align with ISO 31000 principles

Other Key Standards

  • ISO/IEC 20000: IT service management (ITIL alignment)
  • NIST CSF 2.0: Cybersecurity framework (GOVERN function added)
  • SOC 2: Service organization controls (AICPA Trust Services Criteria)
  • PCI-DSS: Payment card industry data security standard

🏛️ IT Governance Structures

  • Board of Directors: Ultimate accountability for IT governance; approves IT risk appetite; oversees major IT investments
  • Audit Committee: Oversees IS auditing function; receives reports on IT risks and controls
  • IT Steering Committee: Business + IT leadership; prioritizes IT investments; monitors IT strategy execution
  • CIO / CISO: Accountable for IT strategy and security governance respectively
  • IT Strategy Committee: Advises board on technology strategy and investment portfolio
  • Risk Committee: Oversees enterprise risk including IT/cyber risks

🎯 IT Strategic Planning Process

  • Align IT strategy with enterprise strategy and mission
  • Assess current IT capabilities and gaps (SWOT, maturity models)
  • Define target IT architecture and investment roadmap
  • Establish IT performance targets and KPIs/KGIs
  • Obtain board approval and communicate to stakeholders
  • Review and update annually (at minimum) or upon significant change
  • Auditor verifies: Is there a documented strategy? Was it board-approved? Is it current?

📊 IT Performance Measurement

The IS auditor evaluates whether IT performance management is rigorous, timely, and linked to business value.

  • KGIs (Key Goal Indicators): Lag indicators — did we achieve the goal? (e.g., system uptime = 99.9%)
  • KPIs (Key Performance Indicators): Lead indicators — are we on track? (e.g., % of patches applied within SLA)
  • Balanced Scorecard: 4 perspectives — Financial, Customer, Internal Process, Learning & Growth
  • IT Scorecard: IT-specific version linking IT activities to business outcomes
  • Metrics must be SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound
  • Benchmarking against industry peers or COBIT capability targets
  • Auditor verifies metrics are not just collected — but acted upon
  • Management dashboards should escalate exceptions automatically
💡

Exam Tip: KGIs measure outcomes (did we succeed?). KPIs measure activities leading to outcomes (are we on track?). The IS auditor prefers KGIs for governance reporting and KPIs for operational monitoring.

Section 05

Enterprise Risk Management

ERM integrates IT risk into the broader organizational risk management program.

🌐 ERM Fundamentals

Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) is the organization-wide process of identifying, assessing, responding to, and monitoring risks across all business functions — including IT.

COSO ERM Framework

COSO ERM (2017 update) integrates risk management with strategy and performance. Five components: Governance & Culture → Strategy & Objective-Setting → Performance → Review & Revision → Information, Communication & Reporting.

⚖️ Risk Response Options

  • Accept (Tolerate): Acknowledge risk; no action taken. Used when cost of mitigation exceeds benefit or risk is within appetite.
  • Avoid (Terminate): Eliminate the risk by discontinuing the activity that creates it.
  • Transfer (Share): Shift financial impact to a third party (insurance, outsourcing, contracts).
  • Mitigate (Treat): Implement controls to reduce likelihood or impact to acceptable level.
⚡

Exam Tip: The IS auditor does NOT make risk response decisions — management does. The auditor evaluates whether responses are appropriate given the risk level and appetite.

Section 06

Enterprise Architecture

🏗️ What is Enterprise Architecture?

Enterprise Architecture (EA) is a strategic framework that describes the structure and operation of an organization, including its business processes, information systems, data, and technology infrastructure. The IS auditor evaluates EA to ensure IT investments are aligned with business strategy and that the architecture is sustainable, secure, and well-governed.

Auditor's Role in EA Reviews

The IS auditor does not design architecture — they evaluate whether: (1) an EA program exists and is formally governed, (2) architectural decisions align with business strategy, (3) security and privacy requirements are built into architectural standards, and (4) deviations from the approved architecture are controlled and documented.

TOGAF (The Open Group Architecture Framework)

  • Most widely adopted EA framework worldwide
  • ADM (Architecture Development Method): Nine-phase iterative cycle for developing EA
  • Four architecture domains: Business, Data, Application, Technology (BDAT)
  • Architecture Repository: stores all EA artifacts and decisions
  • Governance model: Architecture Review Board (ARB) approves deviations
  • Auditor evaluates: Is an ARB in place? Are deviations controlled?

Zachman Framework

  • 6×6 matrix framework — rows: perspectives (Planner to Worker); columns: interrogatives (What, How, Where, Who, When, Why)
  • Descriptive framework — describes what exists, not how to build it
  • Useful for cataloging and classifying EA artifacts
  • Complementary to TOGAF (Zachman describes; TOGAF prescribes how to build)
  • Auditor uses Zachman to ensure completeness of EA coverage across all perspectives

🔒 Security Architecture Considerations

IS auditors evaluate whether security requirements are embedded into the enterprise architecture, not bolted on as an afterthought.

Zero Trust Architecture

Never trust, always verify. Every access request is authenticated and authorized regardless of network location. Microsegmentation, continuous verification, least privilege access.

Defense in Depth

Multiple overlapping layers of controls so that if one fails, others remain. Physical → Network → Application → Data layer controls.

Security by Design

Security requirements defined at the architecture stage — not added later. Includes privacy by design, threat modeling, and secure coding standards.

📂 Data Classification

IS auditors evaluate whether the organization has a formal data classification program that governs how data is handled throughout its lifecycle.

  • Public: No restrictions on disclosure (marketing materials, public reports)
  • Internal: Intended for organizational use; limited external sharing
  • Confidential: Sensitive business data; access need-to-know basis
  • Restricted / Secret: Highest sensitivity; regulatory, legal, or strategic implications
  • Classification must be assigned at creation and reviewed periodically
  • Controls (encryption, access restriction, retention) must match classification level

🔑 Privacy by Design Principles

  • Proactive not reactive: Prevent privacy harms before they occur
  • Privacy as default: Maximum privacy protection without requiring user action
  • Privacy embedded into design: Not an add-on — integral to system design
  • Full functionality: Privacy AND security, not privacy OR security
  • End-to-end security: Throughout the full data lifecycle
  • Visibility and transparency: Operate openly with accountability
  • Respect for user privacy: User-centric, with strong privacy defaults
🎯

Exam Tip: Privacy by Design (PbD) was developed by Ann Cavoukian and has been incorporated into GDPR. When a CISA question involves designing a new system with privacy considerations — PbD principles apply. The auditor evaluates whether these were implemented, not whether they were planned.

Section 08

IT Policies, Standards & Compliance

📋 The Policy Hierarchy

IS auditors evaluate the entire policy framework — not just whether policies exist, but whether they are current, approved, communicated, and enforced.

Hierarchy (Most to Least Authoritative)

Policy (what must be done) → Standard (specific requirements to meet the policy) → Procedure (step-by-step instructions to implement standards) → Guidelines (recommended but not mandatory approaches).

Policy Evaluation Criteria

  • Current: Reviewed and updated within the prescribed cycle (typically annually)
  • Approved: Formally signed off by appropriate authority (Board or senior management)
  • Communicated: Distributed to and acknowledged by all affected parties
  • Enforced: Violations result in documented, consistent consequences
  • Sufficient: Covers all relevant risk areas without material gaps
  • Practical: Can actually be complied with by the intended audience

Key IT Policies the Auditor Reviews

  • Information Security Policy (master policy)
  • Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)
  • Access Control & Password Policy
  • Change Management Policy
  • Incident Response Policy
  • Business Continuity / Disaster Recovery Policy
  • Data Classification and Retention Policy
  • Vendor and Third-Party Risk Management Policy
  • Software Development and Acquisition Policy

🔍 IT Compliance Monitoring

Governance is only effective if compliance is actively monitored and exceptions are escalated and remediated.

  • Continuous control monitoring (automated) vs. periodic reviews (manual)
  • Compliance dashboards with exception reporting to senior management
  • Regulatory change management — track new/amended requirements proactively
  • Third-party compliance: vendor audits, SOC reports, contractual requirements
  • Self-assessments and CSA as supplemental compliance inputs
  • Internal audit as independent compliance validation
  • Disciplinary procedures for policy violations — consistently applied
  • Regulatory liaison function to manage examiner relationships
⚡

Exam Tip: The IS auditor evaluates compliance programs independently — they do not operate them. A common exam trap: "The auditor should implement a compliance monitoring tool." Wrong — management implements; the auditor evaluates.

Real-World Scenarios

Choose the correct auditor action — then reveal the expert explanation.

🏢 Scenario 1 — Board Governance Gap

The Missing IT Strategy

During an IT governance audit, you discover that the organization has no formally documented IT strategic plan. The CIO explains that while IT goals are "aligned with the business" through regular informal conversations with the CEO, nothing has been formally documented or approved by the board. The organization is publicly traded and subject to SOX.

❓ How should the auditor classify and respond to this finding?

A formally documented, board-approved IT strategic plan is a fundamental governance requirement under COBIT 2019 (APO02) and ISO 38500. For a SOX-regulated organization, the absence of formal IT governance documentation is a significant deficiency (or potentially a material weakness) because it impairs the board's ability to exercise oversight. Informal conversations are not a substitute — they are undocumented, uncontrolled, and cannot be independently verified. The auditor should: (1) Rate the finding as High/Significant, (2) Recommend formal documentation with board approval within 90 days, (3) Require evidence of completion — a draft is insufficient to close the finding.

🌍 Scenario 2 — GDPR Compliance Audit

The Data Subject Request Backlog

Your organization operates across the EU and is subject to GDPR. During an audit of the privacy compliance program, you discover that data subject access requests (DSARs) are being responded to within 45–60 days on average. GDPR requires responses within 30 days, with a possible extension to 60 days for complex requests. The privacy team argues that since they rarely exceed 60 days, they are compliant.

❓ What is the auditor's correct assessment?

Under GDPR Article 12(3), the standard response period is 30 days. The extension to 60 days is only permissible for complex or high-volume requests — and the organization MUST notify the data subject within the initial 30 days that an extension has been granted and explain why. Using 45–60 days as a blanket baseline without per-request documentation of complexity is a compliance gap. The auditor should: (1) Confirm there is no per-request extension approval process — this is the finding, (2) Rate the risk based on volume of DSARs and regulatory scrutiny in relevant EU jurisdictions, (3) Recommend implementation of a DSAR tracking system with 30-day SLA and formal extension approval workflow, (4) Note that the absence of complaints does not indicate compliance — regulators conduct proactive investigations.

🏦 Scenario 3 — ERM Governance Gap

IT Risk Outside the Enterprise Framework

During an ERM audit, you discover that the organization has a well-designed enterprise risk management program managed by the Chief Risk Officer (CRO). However, IT-specific risks (cybersecurity threats, system availability, data integrity) are managed entirely separately by the IT department with no integration into the ERM framework. The CRO was unaware of three significant IT risks identified in the most recent IS audit report.

❓ What governance weakness does this represent, and what should the auditor recommend?

This is a fundamental IT governance gap. IT risks are enterprise risks — they affect business operations, financial reporting, reputation, and regulatory compliance. COBIT 2019 (APO12) and COSO ERM both require that IT risk be fully integrated into the enterprise risk management framework. The CRO's inability to report IT risks to the board is a governance failure. The auditor should recommend: (1) Establish a formal IT risk taxonomy aligned with the ERM framework categories, (2) Designate IT risk owners who report into the ERM process, (3) Implement quarterly IT risk reporting to the CRO with a defined escalation threshold for material IT risks, (4) Include the CIO/CISO in the enterprise risk committee, (5) Verify that the next board risk report includes IT risk as a distinct category with appropriate detail.

IT Governance

Board/executive-level direction and oversight of IT

Known ✓

The set of structures, processes, and relational mechanisms that enable the board and management to evaluate, direct, and monitor IT to ensure IT supports and enables business strategy. Distinguished from IT management (execution).

🎯 Exam: Governance = evaluate, direct, monitor (EDM). Management = plan, build, run, monitor (PBRM). Board does governance; CIO does management.

COBIT 2019

ISACA's IT governance and management framework

Known ✓

40 governance and management objectives across 5 domains (EDM, APO, BAI, DSS, MEA). Uses design factors to customize the governance system for the organization. Capability Levels 0–5 measure process maturity. Primary reference framework for CISA exam governance questions.

🎯 Exam: When asked which framework assesses IT governance maturity — COBIT 2019. Capability Level 3 = Established process with consistent performance.

Risk Appetite

Amount of risk an org is willing to accept

Known ✓

The aggregate level and type of risk the organization is willing to accept in pursuit of its business objectives. Set by the board. Distinguished from Risk Tolerance (acceptable deviation from appetite at operational level) and Risk Capacity (maximum risk the org can absorb).

🎯 Exam: Risk appetite is set by the BOARD. Residual risk (after controls) must be accepted by a risk owner with authority commensurate with the risk level.

Enterprise Risk Management (ERM)

Organization-wide risk identification and response

Known ✓

The process of planning, organizing, leading, and controlling the activities of an organization to minimize the effects of risk. COSO ERM and ISO 31000 are leading frameworks. IT risk must be integrated into ERM — not managed as a silo.

🎯 Exam: If IT risks are not integrated into ERM, this is a governance gap — report it as a finding regardless of whether IT manages those risks competently in isolation.

GDPR

EU General Data Protection Regulation

Known ✓

Effective May 2018. Applies to processing of EU/EEA residents' personal data regardless of where the processor is located. Key requirements: lawful basis for processing, data subject rights, 72-hour breach notification, DPO appointment (where required), privacy by design.

🎯 Exam: Max penalty = €20M or 4% of global annual turnover. Breach notification to supervisory authority within 72 hours. Individuals notified if high risk.

Privacy by Design (PbD)

Building privacy into systems at the design stage

Known ✓

7-principle framework developed by Ann Cavoukian. Core idea: privacy protections must be proactively embedded into IT systems and business practices — not bolted on after the fact. Incorporated into GDPR Article 25 as "data protection by design and by default."

🎯 Exam: Auditor verifies PbD by reviewing DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment) records, system design documentation, and developer training programs.

KGI / KPI / KRI

Governance, performance, and risk indicators

Known ✓

KGI (Key Goal Indicator): Lag metric — did we achieve the goal? (e.g., 99.9% uptime achieved). KPI (Key Performance Indicator): Lead metric — are we on track to achieve the goal? KRI (Key Risk Indicator): Early warning signal that risk is increasing (e.g., % of unpatched systems rising).

🎯 Exam: KRIs are proactive — they signal BEFORE a risk event occurs. KGIs measure AFTER. Auditor evaluates whether KRIs trigger management action within defined thresholds.

IT Steering Committee

Joint business-IT body for investment prioritization

Known ✓

A governance body comprising senior business and IT leaders that prioritizes IT investments, monitors major IT projects, resolves cross-functional IT issues, and advises the board on IT strategy. Distinct from the IT Strategy Committee (board-level advisory).

🎯 Exam: Auditor looks for: formal charter, documented meeting minutes, business representation (not just IT), escalation of issues to board. Absence of a steering committee is a governance weakness.

Enterprise Architecture (EA)

Organizational blueprint aligning IT with business

Known ✓

The strategic description of the organization's structure — business processes, information, applications, and technology — and how they interrelate. TOGAF (process-based) and Zachman (classification-based) are the primary frameworks. EA ensures IT investments are aligned, secure, and sustainable.

🎯 Exam: Auditor evaluates: Is an Architecture Review Board (ARB) in place? Are deviations from approved architecture formally controlled? Does EA include security-by-design requirements?

Data Classification

Tiered system of data sensitivity labels

Known ✓

The process of organizing data into categories based on its sensitivity and the business impact if disclosed inappropriately. Common tiers: Public → Internal → Confidential → Restricted. Controls (encryption, access, retention) must be proportionate to classification level.

🎯 Exam: Data owner assigns classification at creation. Auditor verifies: classification policy exists, data is labeled at creation, controls match the classification level, and classification is reviewed periodically.

Risk Tolerance

Acceptable deviation from the risk appetite

Known ✓

The acceptable level of variance around risk objectives — operationalizes the board's risk appetite into specific thresholds management can work within. Example: Risk appetite = "we accept moderate cyber risk." Risk tolerance = "no more than 4 hours of system downtime per month."

🎯 Exam: Risk tolerance translates board-level appetite into operational thresholds. If tolerance is breached, management must escalate and respond — it's not simply logged and accepted.

ITIL v4

IT service management best practices framework

Known ✓

The most widely adopted IT service management framework. Focuses on value co-creation with the Service Value System (SVS) at its core, including the Service Value Chain (6 activities) and 34 management practices. Guiding principle: "Focus on value."

🎯 Exam: ITIL is about SERVICE MANAGEMENT (delivery, support, operations). COBIT is about GOVERNANCE. Distinguish carefully — CISA exam frequently tests this distinction.

Balanced Scorecard

4-perspective strategic performance management tool

Known ✓

Strategic planning tool developed by Kaplan & Norton. Four perspectives: Financial (shareholder value), Customer (satisfaction, retention), Internal Process (efficiency, quality), Learning & Growth (innovation, human capital). IT scorecard is the IT-specific version linking IT activities to each perspective.

🎯 Exam: When assessing IT performance measurement — BSC ensures IT metrics are not only financial/technical but also include customer satisfaction and organizational learning dimensions.

Policy Hierarchy

Policy → Standard → Procedure → Guideline

Known ✓

Policy: High-level mandatory directive (WHAT). Standard: Specific mandatory requirements (HOW MUCH). Procedure: Step-by-step instructions (HOW). Guideline: Recommended but not mandatory approaches (SUGGESTED HOW). Auditors evaluate the entire hierarchy — a well-written policy with no supporting procedures is ineffective.

🎯 Exam: Policies require board/senior management approval. Standards require management approval. The IS auditor verifies the approval level is appropriate to the document type.
